Questions 1–5 · Chapter-exclusive content

1
Gate 1 Chapter only
Chapter 9 identifies four components of the channel value proposition — the offer a supplier must make to convince intermediaries to actively carry and sell its products. Name all four components and give a one-sentence explanation of what each means to a channel partner.
Why this matters: Channel strategy isn't just about moving product — it's about convincing intermediaries that carrying your line is worth their shelf space, capital, and sales effort. The CVP framework is the supplier's answer to the intermediary's question: "Why should I carry your line?"
Chapter 9 · "Channel Value Proposition" section
2
Gate 1 Chapter only
Chapter 9 presents a three-pillar framework for marketing channel performance (Fig. 9-8). Name the three pillars, the outcome each pillar most directly drives, and explain what happens to overall channel performance if any single pillar fails.
Why this matters: A channel can have excellent reach and terrible service quality and still fail. Understanding which pillar drives volume, which drives cost-to-serve, and which drives retention is essential for diagnosing channel problems — and for the case analysis.
Chapter 9 · "Marketing Channel Performance" section · Fig. 9-8
3
Gate 1 Chapter only
Chapter 9 shows that the relationship between a company's distribution outlet share and its market share follows an S-shaped curve (Fig. 9-23). Describe the shape of the curve and explain: (a) at what outlet share level does market share begin to grow faster than outlet share, and (b) why does a dominant outlet share create a competitive advantage that goes beyond just having more points of sale?
Why this matters: In indirect channel markets, distribution access is a strategic asset. A company with 90 dealers doesn't just sell more than a company with 30 — it may also prevent competitors from accessing the best intermediaries at all. The S-curve tells you when adding outlets accelerates returns vs. when it yields diminishing ones.
Chapter 9 · "Distribution Advantage" section · Fig. 9-23
4
Gate 1 Chapter only
Chapter 9 distinguishes between two B2B intermediary types that are unique to business markets: Value-Added Resellers (VARs) and Original Equipment Manufacturers (OEMs). Define each precisely and explain the key difference. Then classify Ford, IBM, and Caterpillar — are they VARs or OEMs, and why?
Why this matters: The VAR/OEM distinction appears on Gate 1 and captures a conceptual line many students blur: bundling existing components for a customer vs. manufacturing a new product from purchased parts. The chapter examples (Ford buying tires, IBM buying processors) make this concrete.
Chapter 9 · "B2B Channels" section
5
Gate 1 Chapter only Calculation
Chapter 9 analyzes a channel of 100 manufacturers and 20,000 retailers with monthly ordering. Work through the math in Figs. 9-21 and 9-22: (a) How many total annual transactions occur without wholesalers? At $50 per transaction, what is the total annual cost? (b) How many total annual transactions occur with wholesalers? What is the total cost? (c) What percentage reduction in total cost does adding wholesalers produce — and what common assumption about intermediaries does this refute?
Why this matters: This is one of the most counterintuitive insights in channel management. Most people assume more intermediaries mean higher costs. The math proves the opposite — and Gate 1 will test whether you have the specific numbers, not just the concept.

Questions 6–8 · Chapter + slides synthesis

6
Gate 2 Calculation
Chapter 9 introduces the pocket price waterfall — the step-by-step deduction of retailer discounts, distributor discounts, agent commissions, and transaction costs from the end-user price. Using the electronic components channel data in Fig. 9-4, trace the pocket price waterfall for all three channels (direct, agent-distributor, and retailer-wholesale). For each channel, show every deduction in order and state the final pocket price. Then compute the weighted average pocket price across all three channels given the sales mix (40% / 20% / 40%).
Why this matters: The pocket price is what the manufacturer actually receives — not the end-user price. Gate 2 will ask you to compute this from scratch with different inputs. The waterfall structure (end-user → retailer cut → distributor cut → commission → transaction cost) is the same across all versions; only the percentages change.

Cross-check: The slides show a simplified version of this using the running shoe example. The chapter's Fig. 9-4 is the full version you need for Gate 2.
Chapter 9 · Figs. 9-4 and 9-5 · Slides S11 (pocket price concept)
7
Gate 2 Calculation
Using the channel profitability data in Fig. 9-5, compute Net Marketing Contribution (NMC), Marketing ROS, and Marketing ROI for the direct and indirect channels. Then answer: which channel has the highest NMC? Which has the highest ROI? Are they the same channel — and if not, why does the channel with the lower NMC have the higher ROI?
Why this matters: This is the module's central paradox. Most students assume the channel with the highest margin and highest NMC is the "best" channel. The ROI calculation proves this is wrong — and understanding why is the difference between a good Gate 2 score and a great case analysis.

Formulas to know:
NMC = (Pocket Price − COGS) × Volume − M&S Expenses ($)
Marketing ROS = NMC ÷ Channel Revenue
Marketing ROI = NMC ÷ M&S Expenses ($)
Chapter 9 · Fig. 9-5 · Slides S12 (channel types)
8
Gate 2 Calculation
Chapter 9's Fig. 9-24 compares three channel scenarios for the same manufacturer: current indirect strategy, direct strategy at the same NMC level, and direct strategy at the same market share. Work through the break-even market share calculation for each scenario. Then answer: under what market share assumption does the direct channel strategy produce a higher NMC than the indirect strategy — and what does the break-even share tell you about the financial risk of each option?
Why this matters: Break-even market share is the single most powerful decision tool in channel strategy. It tells you exactly how much share you can afford to lose before a channel switch destroys value. The case analysis (NovaTech Distribution) will require you to compute this for a proposed channel restructuring.

Formula: Break-Even Market Share (%) = M&S Expense $ ÷ (Pocket Price − COGS) ÷ Market Demand × 100
