Reading Strategy for Chapter 9
📍
Mark the chapter-only figures
Flag Figures 9-8, 9-21, 9-22, 9-23, and 9-24 before you start. Gate 1 pulls from all of these and none are fully covered in the slide deck.
✍️
Write down the formulas
Do not just memorize the names. Next to each formula, write what it measures and which channel decision it supports so the calculation logic is visible when you revisit it.
⚠️
Slow down at the counterintuitive parts
Two places trip students repeatedly: wholesalers can reduce transaction costs, and the channel with the highest NMC can still have the lower ROI. Mark both passages and restate them in your own words.
🔗
Connect the channel metrics to the slides
Chapter 9 links pocket price, Marketing ROI, and break-even market share into one decision sequence. If you map the sequence now, Gate 2 calculation questions become much easier.
Eight Focus Questions — Answer These While You Read
1
Gate 1 · Chapter Only
Channel value proposition
Chapter 9 identifies four components of the channel value proposition — the offer a supplier must make to convince intermediaries to actively carry and sell its products. Name all four components and give a one-sentence explanation of what each means to a channel partner.
📄 Chapter 9 · Channel Value Proposition section
Remember that channels ask what is in it for them. The four components are the supplier's answer.
2
Gate 1 · Chapter Only
Channel performance triad
Chapter 9 presents a three-pillar framework for marketing channel performance (Fig. 9-8). Name the three pillars, the outcome each pillar most directly drives, and explain what happens to overall channel performance if any single pillar fails.
📄 Chapter 9 · Fig. 9-8
Think of the triad as reach, efficiency, and service. Gate 1 expects the full logic, not just the labels.
3
Gate 1 · Chapter Only
Distribution outlet share S-curve
Chapter 9 shows that the relationship between a company's distribution outlet share and its market share follows an S-shaped curve (Fig. 9-23). Describe the shape of the curve and explain: (a) at what outlet share level does market share begin to grow faster than outlet share, and (b) why a dominant outlet share creates a competitive advantage that goes beyond just having more points of sale?
📄 Chapter 9 · Fig. 9-23
The key idea is that access to quality outlets can block rivals, not just expand your own volume.
4
Gate 1 · Chapter Only
VARs versus OEMs
Chapter 9 distinguishes between two B2B intermediary types that are unique to business markets: Value-Added Resellers (VARs) and Original Equipment Manufacturers (OEMs). Define each precisely and explain the key difference. Then classify Ford, IBM, and Caterpillar — are they VARs or OEMs, and why?
📄 Chapter 9 · B2B Channels section
The distinction is about what gets assembled or transformed, and who is responsible for the final product identity.
5
Gate 1 · Chapter Only · Calculation
Wholesaler transaction math
Chapter 9 analyzes a channel of 100 manufacturers and 20,000 retailers with monthly ordering. Work through the math in Figs. 9-21 and 9-22: (a) How many total annual transactions occur without wholesalers? At $50 per transaction, what is the total annual cost? (b) How many total annual transactions occur with wholesalers? What is the total cost? (c) What percentage reduction in total cost does adding wholesalers produce — and what common assumption about intermediaries does this refute?
📄 Chapter 9 · Figs. 9-21 and 9-22
The math is the point: more intermediaries can reduce transaction cost dramatically when they reduce the number of transactions.
6
Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides
Pocket price waterfall
Chapter 9 introduces the pocket price waterfall — the step-by-step deduction of retailer discounts, distributor discounts, agent commissions, and transaction costs from the end-user price. Using the electronic components channel data in Fig. 9-4, trace the pocket price waterfall for all three channels (direct, agent-distributor, and retailer-wholesale). For each channel, show every deduction in order and state the final pocket price. Then compute the weighted average pocket price across all three channels given the sales mix (40% / 20% / 40%).
📄 Chapter 9 · Figs. 9-4 and 9-5 · Slides S11
The pocket price is what the manufacturer actually receives. The waterfall structure is the same each time; only the percentages change.
7
Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides
Channel profitability paradox
Using the channel profitability data in Fig. 9-5, compute Net Marketing Contribution (NMC), Marketing ROS, and Marketing ROI for the direct and indirect channels. Then answer: which channel has the highest NMC? Which has the highest ROI? Are they the same channel — and if not, why does the channel with the lower NMC have the higher ROI?
📄 Chapter 9 · Fig. 9-5 · Slides S12
The central paradox is that ROI depends on M&S spending efficiency, not just absolute profit.
8
Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides
Break-even market share
Chapter 9's Fig. 9-24 compares three channel scenarios for the same manufacturer: current indirect strategy, direct strategy at the same NMC level, and direct strategy at the same market share. Work through the break-even market share calculation for each scenario. Then answer: under what market share assumption does the direct channel strategy produce a higher NMC than the indirect strategy — and what does the break-even share tell you about the financial risk of each option?
📄 Chapter 9 · Fig. 9-24 · Profit Impact of Alternative Marketing Channels
Use the break-even share formula to compare the downside risk of channel redesign decisions.
🎯 Highest-Probability Gate 1 Targets
If you are short on time, prioritize these three areas — they appear on Gate 1 across all question variants and cannot be answered from the slides alone:
  • Channel value proposition (Q1): Know the four components and why intermediaries need each one.
  • Channel performance and outlet share (Q2 & Q3): Be able to explain the triad and the S-curve without looking at the figure.
  • Wholesaler transaction math (Q5): Be able to compute why adding wholesalers reduces total transaction cost so dramatically.
