Purpose

Chapter 7 covers more ground than the slide deck. Some of that additional content is tested in Gate 1 — questions that cannot be answered from slides alone. Other content is tested in Gate 2, which requires combining your reading with what you see in the slides.

This guide tells you exactly what to look for while you read. Work through these questions as you go — not after. Students who treat this as a checklist to skim after finishing the chapter do significantly worse on Gate 1.

Gate 2 — After Slides

Reading Strategy
🗺️
Read with these questions open

Keep this page alongside your textbook. When you hit the relevant section, stop and answer the question before moving on.

📊
Spend extra time on figures

Fig 7-3, Fig 7-21, Fig 7-22, and Fig 7-24 are all tested numerically. Read their notes and column labels, not just the visual.

🔢
Write down the numbers

Gate 2 includes numeric calculations. The Samsung financials, the scorecard weights, and the ROA table values need to be in your notes — not just your memory.

⚠️
Watch for the counterintuitive

Two results in Chapter 7 are the opposite of what most people expect. Questions 3 and 5 below point directly at them. When you hit those moments, slow down.

The Eight Questions
  • 1
    Gate 1 · Chapter Only
    Samsung repositioned its entire brand after recording a $800M loss in 1997. What specific financial results did the repositioning produce by 2001 and 2005? And what does the chapter say had to change for the repositioning to work?
    📄 pp. 233–234, Fig 7-5 Write down the exact sales and profit margin figures for all three years.
  • 2
    Gate 1 · Chapter Only
    The Brand Assets Scorecard in Figure 7-21 has five dimensions. What are they, what weight does each carry, and what does an overall score of 50 mean relative to competitors?
    📄 pp. 252–253, Fig 7-21 List all five dimensions by name. Note the weight and the 0–100 scale anchor points.
  • 3
    Gate 1 · Chapter Only · ⚠️ Counterintuitive
    Figure 7-24 shows ROA for narrow, average, and broad product lines across three lifecycle stages. Fill in this table from the figure: What is the ROA for each line width at the Mature stage? Why is the result surprising?
    📄 p. 256, Fig 7-24 Narrow = ?, Average = ?, Broad = ? at Mature. The surprising part: broad and narrow are identical. Average is lower than both.
  • 4
    Gate 1 · Chapter Only
    Loctite repositioned RC-601 as Quick Metal after the original product failed. What was the original target customer, price, and ad message? What changed in the repositioning — and why did the price go up when the new target is a lower-level employee?
    📄 pp. 231–232, Fig 7-3 Use Figure 7-3's six-row table. The price-increase question is the key analytical move in this case.
  • 5
    Gate 1 · Chapter Only · ⚠️ Counterintuitive
    What is the difference between pure bundling and mixed bundling? Which strategy does the chapter say produces higher perceived value and higher profitability — and what does that imply about when to use each?
    📄 pp. 260–261 The research finding on profitability is the opposite of what most managers assume. Write it down explicitly.
  • 6
    Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides
    The Brand Assets Scorecard and Brand Liabilities Scorecard both use the same weighted scoring method. Practice the calculation: if a brand rates Above Average (75) on Brand Awareness and About Average (50) on the other four asset dimensions, what is the total Brand Assets Score?
    📄 pp. 252–255, Figs 7-21, 7-22, 7-23 Each dimension = rating × 20%. Sum the five products. Gate 2 Q1 and Q2 ask you to do this with different numbers.
  • 7
    Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides
    The chapter describes five ways to create a brand name: Founder, Functional, Invented, Experiential, and Evocative. For each category, write down one example from the chapter and your own example not in the chapter. What distinguishes each category from the others?
    📄 pp. 248–250, Fig 7-18 · Also: Slide 38 (updated taxonomy) Gate 2 Q5 gives you a brand name and asks you to classify it. The distinctions between categories — especially Invented vs. Experiential vs. Evocative — are where students lose points.
  • 8
    Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides
    The chapter identifies five brand liability dimensions. For each one, name a real company or event that illustrates it — and explain what specific management action could have reduced the liability before it became acute.
    📄 pp. 253–254, Fig 7-22 Gate 2 Q8 gives you a scenario (recall, scandal, ESG failure) and asks which liability dimension is most affected and what the right response is. This question prepares you for that judgment.
⚑ Before you attempt Gate 1

Make sure you can answer Questions 1–5 from memory, without opening the book. Gate 1 questions are written so that students who only skimmed the chapter will not recognize the correct answer. The Samsung financials, the Fig 7-24 ROA table, and the Loctite before/after table are the three highest-probability targets. If you are uncertain about any of those, re-read those sections before proceeding.
