Reading Strategy for Chapter 7
📍
Mark the chapter-only figures
Flag Figure 7-3, Figure 7-21, Figure 7-22, and Figure 7-24 before you start. Gate 1 and Gate 2 both pull from these tables and scorecards.
✍️
Write the numbers with the labels
Do not just memorize the outcome. Note the Samsung financials, the Brand Assets Scorecard weights, and the Mature-stage ROA values so the chapter logic is visible when you revisit them.
⚠️
Slow down at the counterintuitive parts
Two places trip students repeatedly: the Chapter 7 ROA pattern across line widths and the finding that mixed bundling can outperform pure bundling. Mark both passages and restate them in your own words.
🔗
Connect the taxonomy to the slides
Chapter 7 links brand assets, brand naming, and brand liabilities into one decision sequence. If you map how those tools fit together now, Gate 2 best-answer questions become much easier.
Eight Focus Questions — Answer These While You Read
1
Gate 1 · Chapter Only
Samsung repositioning and financial outcomes
Samsung repositioned its entire brand after recording a $800M loss in 1997. What specific financial results did the repositioning produce by 2001 and 2005? And what does the chapter say had to change for the repositioning to work?
📄 pp. 233–234, Fig. 7-5
Write down the exact sales and profit margin figures for all three years.
2
Gate 1 · Chapter Only
Brand Assets Scorecard dimensions and weights
The Brand Assets Scorecard in Figure 7-21 has five dimensions. What are they, what weight does each carry, and what does an overall score of 50 mean relative to competitors?
📄 pp. 252–253, Fig. 7-21
List all five dimensions by name. Note the weight and the 0–100 scale anchor points.
3
Gate 1 · Chapter Only · ⚠️ Counterintuitive
ROA by line width in the Mature stage
Figure 7-24 shows ROA for narrow, average, and broad product lines across three lifecycle stages. Fill in this table from the figure: What is the ROA for each line width at the Mature stage? Why is the result surprising?
📄 p. 256, Fig. 7-24
Narrow = ?, Average = ?, Broad = ? at Mature. The surprising part: broad and narrow are identical. Average is lower than both.
4
Gate 1 · Chapter Only
Loctite / Quick Metal repositioning
Loctite repositioned RC-601 as Quick Metal after the original product failed. What was the original target customer, price, and ad message? What changed in the repositioning — and why did the price go up when the new target is a lower-level employee?
📄 pp. 231–232, Fig. 7-3
Use Figure 7-3's six-row table. The price-increase question is the key analytical move in this case.
5
Gate 1 · Chapter Only · ⚠️ Counterintuitive
Pure bundling versus mixed bundling
What is the difference between pure bundling and mixed bundling? Which strategy does the chapter say produces higher perceived value and higher profitability — and what does that imply about when to use each?
📄 pp. 260–261
The research finding on profitability is the opposite of what most managers assume. Write it down explicitly.
6
Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides
Brand Assets Scorecard calculation
The Brand Assets Scorecard and Brand Liabilities Scorecard both use the same weighted scoring method. Practice the calculation: if a brand rates Above Average (75) on Brand Awareness and About Average (50) on the other four asset dimensions, what is the total Brand Assets Score?
📄 pp. 252–255, Figs. 7-21, 7-22, 7-23
Each dimension = rating × 20%. Sum the five products. Gate 2 Q1 and Q2 ask you to do this with different numbers.
7
Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides
Brand naming categories
The chapter describes five ways to create a brand name: Founder, Functional, Invented, Experiential, and Evocative. For each category, write down one example from the chapter and your own example not in the chapter. What distinguishes each category from the others?
📄 pp. 248–250, Fig. 7-18 · Also: Slide 38
Gate 2 Q5 gives you a brand name and asks you to classify it. The distinctions between categories are where students lose points.
8
Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides
Brand liability dimensions
The chapter identifies five brand liability dimensions. For each one, name a real company or event that illustrates it — and explain what specific management action could have reduced the liability before it became acute.
📄 pp. 253–254, Fig. 7-22
Gate 2 Q8 gives you a scenario and asks which liability dimension is most affected and what the right response is. This question prepares you for that judgment.
🎯 Highest-Probability Gate 1 Targets
If you are short on time, prioritize these three areas — they appear on Gate 1 across all question variants and cannot be answered from the slides alone:
  • Samsung financials (Q1): Know the exact change in sales and profit margin across 1997, 2001, and 2005, and what had to change internally for the repositioning to work.
  • Mature-stage ROA pattern (Q3): The chapter's result is counterintuitive: broad and narrow are identical, and average is lower than both. Memorize the pattern, not just the figure title.
  • Loctite before/after table (Q4): Be able to describe what changed in target, message, and pricing when RC-601 became Quick Metal.
