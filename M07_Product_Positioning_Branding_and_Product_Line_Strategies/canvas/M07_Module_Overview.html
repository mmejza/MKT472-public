Product Positioning, Branding, and Product Line Strategies

A stronger product does not automatically produce a stronger brand. This module explains why — and what to do about it.

Part III · Marketing Mix Strategies 195 points Two-Gate Accountability
Central Question
What this module asks you to figure out
Why do some companies with genuinely superior products still lose market share — and what does brand equity have to do with it?

This module builds the analytical toolkit for product positioning and brand management: the Customer Value Hierarchy, Points of Parity and Differentiation, the Brand Assets and Liabilities Scorecards, the Brand Equity calculation, and the product line breadth‑vs.‑ROA tradeoff from Figure 7-24. Each tool is straightforward on its own. The challenge is applying them together to reach a strategic conclusion that the numbers actually support.

The central intellectual challenge is the brand equity paradox: a company can invest heavily in brand assets — real quality improvements, real repositioning effort, real marketing spend — and still watch brand equity decline if liabilities are accumulating faster than assets are being built. Samsung’s 1997–2005 repositioning ($22.6B in sales and a −$800M loss in 1997; $85.4B and +$8B by 2005) is the chapter’s proof case. That data is chapter-exclusive and Gate 1‑tested.

The case company, Apex Athletic, presents a brand equity position that appears healthy at first glance — and reveals itself as fragile under the liability spike scenario. That is the analytical trap this module is designed to help you avoid.

!
Analytical framing

Brand Equity = Brand Assets Score − Brand Liabilities Score. Positive equity does not mean safe equity. A brand with a Customer Dissatisfaction liability at Above Average (75) is one event away from a materially different position. The case forces you to calculate exactly how different.

Learning Objectives

Six objectives mapped to Bloom’s Taxonomy. All six are testable and appear in gate questions, app worksheets, or the case analysis.

LO1
Analysis
Trace the causal chain from weak product positioning through margin erosion, share loss, and declining brand equity; identify where differentiation investment interrupts that chain.
LO2
Application
Apply the Customer Value Hierarchy to classify product attributes as Core Benefit, Expected, Augmented, or Potential; distinguish Points of Parity from Points of Differentiation for a given competitive set.
LO3
Application
Calculate a Brand Assets Score and Brand Liabilities Score using the weighted scorecard (20% per dimension, 0–100 scale); compute Brand Equity and interpret the strategic implication of the assets-to-liabilities ratio.
LO4
Analysis
Classify brand growth strategies (line extension, brand extension, fighter brand, new brand) given a scenario; evaluate the conditions under which each strategy builds versus erodes brand equity.
LO5
Evaluation
Assess a product line breadth decision by applying the ROA-by-lifecycle-stage data from Figure 7-24; identify when broad lines outperform narrow lines and when the advantage reverses at the Mature stage.
LO6
Synthesis
Construct a repositioning recommendation that integrates differentiation source, brand encoding strategy, product line implications, and financial justification; ground the recommendation in case-specific data, not generic marketing language.

Two Traps to Watch For

This module contains two specific strategic errors that students make consistently. Both appear in the lecture video and the apps. Know them before you start the case.

Trap 1 — Treating Positive Brand Equity as Safe Brand Equity

Apex Athletic’s Brand Equity is +30. That is positive. Most students stop there and treat the brand as secure. The trap is that +30 with Customer Dissatisfaction already at Above Average (75) is one event away from a materially lower equity position. When Customer Dissatisfaction spikes to Very High (100), equity drops from +30 to +25 — and further simultaneous liability exposure pushes it lower still.

The CFO sees +30 and concludes the brand needs no defense. The Brand Manager sees the liability trend and concludes the opposite. Both are looking at the same scorecard. One is reading it more carefully.

Where to find it: Lecture Segment 1 (the brand liabilities trap), App 2 Tab C spike simulator, and Case Part 1 vulnerability analysis.

Trap 2 — Assuming Product Line Reduction Improves ROA at the Mature Stage

When a manager wants to “streamline” a mature, broad product line, the instinct is that cutting underperforming lines will improve ROA. Figure 7-24 shows the opposite: at the Mature stage, broad and narrow lines both produce 23% ROA. Reducing from Broad to Average produces only 18% — a five percentage-point decline that translates to a $24M annual profit reduction on $480M in revenue.

Students who recommend the line reduction without running the Fig 7-24 numbers lose points on Case Part 3 regardless of how well they articulate a rationale.

Where to find it: Chapter 7 p. 256 Fig 7-24, App 3 Tab A (Apex Athletic preset), and Case Part 3.
