Background

Apex Athletic has been the leading mid-price athletic footwear brand for a decade. A new competitor has entered the market at a lower price point and is eroding Apex’s share in the value segment. Two executives are looking at the same brand equity scorecard and reaching opposite conclusions about how to respond.

The CFO — Stay the Course

“Our brand assets score is strong at 72. Brand equity is +30 — that’s positive, and it’s been stable. Defending against the low-price entrant would require either launching a fighter brand ($4.2M Year 1 cost) or discounting our core line (−8% margin). Neither option makes financial sense when equity is already in our favor. We should stay the course and protect margin.”

The Brand Manager — Launch the Fighter Brand

“You’re reading the scorecard wrong. Yes, equity is +30 now — but our Customer Dissatisfaction score is already at 75, the highest liability dimension. If that spikes to Very High (100), equity falls to +25. We’re one product failure away from a materially different position. The $4.2M fighter brand is not a luxury — it’s the floor of our defense. Waiting is how brands lose equity they can’t rebuild.”

What “Specific Data” Means

The best posts cite at least two specific values from the module — the +30 Brand Equity figure, the CD score of 75, the $4.2M fighter brand cost, the 23% vs. 18% ROA comparison, or the spike scenario result. Saying “the liabilities are rising” is not a data-supported argument. Saying “a Customer Dissatisfaction score of 75 is the highest single liability dimension, and the spike simulator shows equity falls from +30 to +25 if it reaches Very High” is a data-supported argument. The numbers matter.
