Creating Brand Names
Five categories — Gate 1 tests all five with definitions and examples
1. Founder Names
Named for company founders. Brand equity built without benefit association — recognition depends on founder reputation.
Examples: Dell (Michael Dell), Wal-Mart (Sam Walton), Ford (Henry Ford)
2. Functional Names
Derived from the core benefit or function. Build direct function–brand association — the name communicates what the product does.
Examples: Duracell (long lasting), FedEx (fast delivery), Microsoft (microcomputer software)
3. Invented Names
Built from root words, morphemes, or poetic constructions. Highly distinctive and protectable — no prior meaning to compete with.
Examples: Kleenex, Google (from "googol"), Oreo, Snapple
4. Experiential Names
Associated with an experience or feeling of discovery, success, or movement. Name evokes the usage experience rather than the product itself.
Examples: Land Rover (exploration), Big Bertha (power), Kodak EasyShare (ease of use)
5. Evocative Names
Evoke a positive attribute, emotion, or feeling. Name creates an association rather than communicating function or experience directly.
Examples: Apple (friendly, easy to use), Victoria's Secret (seductive), Virgin Airlines (excitement, novelty)
