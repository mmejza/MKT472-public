Background

A regional outdoor apparel company has completed a needs-based market segmentation study. Three segments have been identified:

Segment Market Size Annual Growth # Competitors Company Revenue Share Marketing ROI
Performance Seekers 12% of market 22%/yr 3 8% of company revenue 210%
Casual Buyers 58% of market 2%/yr 28 70% of company revenue 43%
Sustainability-Focused 30% of market 14%/yr 11 22% of company revenue ~130%

The company has served Casual Buyers for 15 years. Its sales force, retail relationships, and service infrastructure are all calibrated around that segment. Casual Buyers represent 70% of current company revenue.

The Leadership Debate

After reviewing the segmentation data, the VP of Sales and the VP of Marketing have reached sharply different conclusions. Both are working from the same numbers.

VP of Sales
Defend the current segment

"We built this company on Casual Buyers. Walking away from 70% of our revenue is reckless. Yes, our Marketing ROI is lower there than in Performance Seekers — but the absolute revenue is real. Abandoning loyal customers and established retail relationships to chase a smaller segment is not a strategy. It is a gamble."

VP of Marketing
Redirect toward higher ROI

"We are not walking away from revenue. We are walking away from a trap. The health insurance company in Chapter 5 stayed in their lowest-value segment too long and spent years unwinding the damage. Our Marketing ROI in Casual Buyers is 43%. In Performance Seekers it is 210%. Every dollar we invest in Casual Buyers returns less than a quarter of what the same dollar would return in Performance Seekers. That is not loyalty — that is a misallocation."

Your Task

Part 1 (150–200 words). Take a clear position — side with either the VP of Sales or the VP of Marketing. Defend your position using:

  • At least two specific attractiveness factors from the segment data above
  • At least one profitability metric (Marketing ROI, NMC, or revenue)
  • Specific numbers — not general claims

Part 2 — Devil's Advocate (100–150 words). Now make the strongest case for the position you just argued against. Use specific data from this module to support the opposing argument. What is the best evidence the other side has? Where is your own argument most vulnerable?

What a Strong Post Looks Like

Strong Posts Will
  1. State a clear position in the first sentence — not "on one hand… on the other hand."
  2. Cite at least two attractiveness factors by name with the specific numbers from the segment table (e.g., "Casual Buyers has 28 competitors and only 2% annual growth…").
  3. Apply a profitability metric correctly — compare the 43% ROI in Casual Buyers to the 210% ROI in Performance Seekers and explain what the difference means for budget allocation.
  4. Write a genuine devil's advocate section — make the strongest possible case against your own position using specific numbers from the segment table. If you sided with the VP of Marketing, the VP of Sales has a real point about revenue concentration risk. If you sided with the VP of Sales, the ROI differential (210% vs. 43%) is the opposing side's strongest weapon. Identify your own argument's most vulnerable point.
  5. Apply the Chapter 5 health insurance parallel if siding with the VP of Marketing — explain why high share in a low-ROI segment is a liability rather than a moat.
