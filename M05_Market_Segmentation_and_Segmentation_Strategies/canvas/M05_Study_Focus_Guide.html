What This Guide Does

Chapter 5 covers more than the slide deck and lecture video. Gate 1 tests five topics that appear only in the chapter — not in the slides and not in the script. If you skim or skip the reading, you will not have the information you need to pass Gate 1 and unlock the slides.

Gate 2 adds a synthesis layer: it tests three constructs that require combining what you read in the chapter with what you see in the slides. The questions in this guide are divided by gate, so you know exactly what to look for as you read and which questions require you to also study the slides.

Use this guide while you read — not after. Write down your answers as you find them. These are the same constructs that will appear in Gate 1, Gate 2, and the Apex Athletic case analysis.

Reading Strategy — Chapter 5 Specifics
📐
Record the exact numbers
Several Gate 1 questions are built around specific data points in the chapter — revenue indexes, profit multiples, segment percentages. Write down any figure that includes a precise number (e.g., "729 combinations," "revenue index 100 vs. 181," "16 times"). These are the details the slides do not repeat.
🗂️
Study every figure and table
Chapter 5 is figure-dense. Fig. 5-6, 5-7, 5-10, 5-11, and 5-12 all contain Gate-tested content that does not appear on the slides. For each figure, ask: what is the key distinction or data relationship this figure is showing?
⚠️
Flag counterintuitive results
Two questions in this guide are marked Counterintuitive — the chapter result contradicts common intuition. Slow down when you encounter these. They are the highest-probability Gate 1 targets and the most common source of errors on the case analysis.
🔗
Questions 6–8 require both sources
The last three questions in this guide (Gate 2 targets) cannot be answered from the chapter alone. After you view the slides, return to Questions 6–8 and verify your answers using the combined chapter + slide content before taking Gate 2.

    Gate 1 · Chapter Only 📍 pp. 163–164
    The chapter describes a specific error called "the demographic trap." If a marketer uses six segmentation variables with three categories each, how many possible segment combinations does this produce — and why does the chapter argue this number is strategically useless?
    Record the exact number of combinations (formula: 36) and the chapter's explanation of why the correct segmentation sequence starts with needs, not demographics.
  • 2
    Gate 1 · Chapter Only
    Gate 1 · Chapter Only 📍 Fig. 5-11 · pp. 167–168 ⚠️ Counterintuitive
    In the health insurance market segmentation case (Figure 5-11), the company held 40% market share in Segment I (Insurance Minimizers) and only 19% in Segment III (Premium Buyers). The revenue index for Insurance Minimizers is 100; for Premium Buyers it is 181. What does this pattern reveal about the strategic error the company had been making — and why is high market share not evidence of good targeting?
    This result is counterintuitive: the company was strongest exactly where it generated the least value per customer. Record the three revenue index values (Segments I, II, III) and write one sentence explaining the strategic implication.
  • 3
    Gate 1 · Chapter Only
    Gate 1 · Chapter Only 📍 pp. 181–184
    Chapter 5 defines three distinct customer relationship marketing programs: mass personalization, mass customization, and customer relationship management (in the narrow sense). What is the defining characteristic that distinguishes each type — and what specific condition (involving both customer value and company value) must be met before a firm is justified in using the full CRM program?
    Record the one-sentence definition of each program type, and the exact dual-condition threshold for CRM. The American Express and Marriott examples in the chapter illustrate which level applies to each company.
  • 4
    Gate 1 · Chapter Only
    Gate 1 · Chapter Only 📍 pp. 186–187
    Chapter 5 defines customer touch points and distinguishes two types: direct and indirect. What are three examples of direct touch points and two examples of indirect touch points — and why does the chapter describe indirect touch points as "sometimes even more important" than direct ones?
    List the examples the chapter gives for each type. The chapter's explanation of why indirect touch points outperform direct ones in acquiring new customers is a key testable claim — write it in your own words.

    Gate 1 · Chapter Only 📍 Fig. 5-6 · pp. 161–162 · Fig. 5-7
    Figure 5-6 shows three forces that shape business-to-business customer needs, parallel to the three forces that shape consumer needs. What are the three B2B forces, what are the specific sub-factors listed under each, and which consumer force does each B2B force parallel? Then, from Figure 5-7: what are the two core segments of the small business market, and what distinguishes them?
    Draw the three-column structure of Fig. 5-6 in your notes. For Fig. 5-7, record the two segment names (growth-oriented entrepreneurs vs. cost-focused sustainers) and the two or three characteristics that most clearly separate them.

    Figure 5-10 lists the three forces that determine segment attractiveness and the sub-factors under each. For a hypothetical segment with a large current market size but 38 direct competitors, low entry barriers, and poor company channel access — which attractiveness force most clearly makes this segment unattractive, and which specific sub-factors drive that judgment?
    Record all nine sub-factors from Fig. 5-10 (three per force). Then apply them to the scenario: identify which force scores lowest and which two sub-factors are decisive. This is the exact reasoning structure Gate 2 Q2 tests.

    Gate 2 · Ch + Slides
    Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides 📍 pp. 163–165 · Fig. 5-8
    A product team segmented its market by purchase frequency (daily, weekly, occasional buyers) and built separate retention programs for each group. Six months later, retention rates were nearly identical across all three groups. Using the 7-step needs-based segmentation process from Figure 5-8, which specific step did the team skip — and what consequence does skipping that step produce?
    Figure 5-8 lists all seven steps in order. The answer requires identifying both the skipped step by name and explaining the mechanism: segments formed without Step 1 may be identifiable but not differentiable — meaning they do not respond differently to different programs.
  • 8
    Gate 2 · Ch + Slides
    Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides 📍 Fig. 5-12 · pp. 168–170 ⚠️ Counterintuitive
    Figure 5-12 shows the segment profitability analysis for three silicon sealant segments: Engineered Solution, Service Solution, and Price Solution. Using the NMC, Marketing ROS, and Marketing ROI values from the figure: which segment has the highest absolute NMC — and which segment has the highest Marketing ROI? Are they the same segment, and what does any difference imply for a firm with a limited marketing budget?
    Work through the NMC formula for all three segments (NMC = Demand × Share × Price × Margin − M&S Expense). Then calculate Marketing ROI = NMC ÷ M&S Expense for each. The counterintuitive result: the segment with the highest NMC does not have the highest ROI. Record both rankings and the strategic implication for a resource-constrained firm.
Highest-Probability Gate 1 Targets
⚡ Focus Here First — These Three Are Most Likely to Appear
Based on the module's Gate 1 content map, these topics have the highest probability of appearing in your specific version of Gate 1. The chapter covers all five focus areas, but these three are the most precisely testable — and the most frequently missed by students who skim:
  • The health insurance case (Fig. 5-11, pp. 167–168). Know the exact revenue index values for all three segments and be able to explain why the firm's highest-share segment was its least-valuable one. This is the counterintuitive result most students miss on first read.
  • The CRM program types (pp. 181–184). Know the precise definition of each of the three programs and the specific dual-condition that triggers full CRM. The chapter's examples (American Express, United Airlines, Marriott) are designed to make the distinctions concrete — use them.
  • The B2B segmentation forces (Fig. 5-6, pp. 161–162). Know which of the three B2B forces corresponds to which consumer segmentation force. The parallel structure is the exact pattern Gate 1 tests — a scenario description of a B2B customer need, and you must identify which force is shaping it.
