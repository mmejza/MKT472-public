Reading Strategy for Chapter 5
📐
Record exact numbers
Several Gate 1 targets hinge on precise chapter data, including combination counts, revenue indexes, and segment comparisons. When the chapter gives a number, record it exactly instead of paraphrasing the result.
🗂️
Study the figures, not just the prose
Figures 5-6, 5-10, 5-11, and 5-12 carry several of the most testable ideas in the module. Read each figure as an argument about relationships, not as decoration around the text.
⚠️
Pause at counterintuitive results
The chapter uses at least two results that run against intuition: high share in the wrong segment and the difference between highest NMC and highest ROI. Those are common failure points because students read past the strategic implication.
🔗
Reconnect questions 6–8 after slides
The last three questions rely on synthesis between the chapter and slide deck. Use the chapter to define the structure first, then come back after the slides to verify which factor or metric actually drives the decision.
Eight Focus Questions — Answer These While You Read
1
The Demographic Trap
Gate 1 · Chapter Only
pp. 163–164
The chapter describes a specific error called the demographic trap. If a marketer uses six segmentation variables with three categories each, how many possible segment combinations does this produce, and why does the chapter argue that this number is strategically useless?
Record the exact number of combinations and the chapter's explanation of why the correct sequence starts with needs rather than demographics.
2
Health Insurance Pivot Case
Gate 1 · Chapter Only ⚠️ Counterintuitive
Fig. 5-11 · pp. 167–168
In the health insurance segmentation case, the company held 40% market share in Insurance Minimizers but only 19% in Premium Buyers. If the revenue index for Insurance Minimizers is 100 and the revenue index for Premium Buyers is 181, what strategic error had the company been making, and why is high market share not evidence of good targeting?
Record the revenue index pattern for all three segments and write one sentence explaining why strength in the least valuable segment is a liability rather than a success.
3
CRM Program Types
Gate 1 · Chapter Only
pp. 181–184
Chapter 5 defines three distinct customer relationship marketing programs: mass personalization, mass customization, and customer relationship management in the narrow sense. What distinguishes each type, and what exact condition involving both customer value and company value must be met before full CRM is justified?
Write a one-sentence definition for each program type and capture the chapter's dual-condition threshold for true CRM.
4
Customer Touch Points
Gate 1 · Chapter Only
pp. 186–187
Chapter 5 distinguishes direct from indirect customer touch points. What are three examples of direct touch points, what are two examples of indirect touch points, and why does the chapter argue that indirect touch points can be even more important than direct ones?
List the examples by type and then write the chapter's explanation for why indirect touch points can be more effective in attracting new customers.
5
B2B Segmentation Forces
Gate 1 · Chapter Only
Fig. 5-6 · pp. 161–162 · Fig. 5-7
Figure 5-6 shows three forces that shape business-to-business customer needs, parallel to the three forces that shape consumer needs. What are the three B2B forces, what sub-factors sit under each, and what are the two small-business segments in Figure 5-7 and the distinction between them?
Draw the three-force structure from Figure 5-6 and connect each to its consumer-side parallel. Then record the two small-business segment names and what separates them.

Segment Attractiveness Forces
Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides
Fig. 5-10 · pp. 165–167
Figure 5-10 lists the three forces that determine segment attractiveness and the sub-factors under each. For a hypothetical segment with large current size but many direct competitors, low entry barriers, and weak company channel access, which attractiveness force most clearly makes the segment unattractive, and which specific sub-factors drive that judgment?
Record all nine sub-factors from the figure, then use them to justify which force scores lowest. Gate 2 tests this reasoning pattern more than memorized labels alone.
7
Needs-Based Segmentation Process
Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides
pp. 163–165 · Fig. 5-8
A product team segmented its market by purchase frequency and built separate retention programs for each group, but six months later retention rates were nearly identical across all groups. Using the 7-step needs-based segmentation process in Figure 5-8, which step did the team skip, and what consequence does skipping that step produce?
Figure 5-8 must be understood in sequence. Identify the skipped step by name and explain how skipping it creates segments that are identifiable but not meaningfully differentiable.
8
NMC vs. Marketing ROI by Segment
Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides ⚠️ Counterintuitive
Fig. 5-12 · pp. 168–170
Figure 5-12 shows the segment profitability analysis for three silicon sealant segments. Which segment has the highest absolute Net Marketing Contribution, which segment has the highest Marketing ROI, are they the same segment, and what does any difference imply for a firm facing a limited marketing budget?
Work through both rankings separately. The key insight is that the segment generating the most contribution dollars may not be the segment generating the highest return on each marketing dollar invested.
⚠️
High-Risk Reading Traps
Three Chapter 5 ideas create the most mistakes unless you write down the logic as you read:
  • Demographics are not a valid starting point by themselves: the chapter's 729-combination example exists to show why tidy categories can still be strategically meaningless.
  • High share can hide bad targeting: the health insurance case shows that a firm can dominate the wrong segment and still underperform strategically.
  • Highest NMC and highest ROI are not always the same segment: when resources are constrained, return efficiency can matter more than raw contribution volume.
