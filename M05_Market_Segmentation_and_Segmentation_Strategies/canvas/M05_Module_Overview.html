Module Overview

This module develops the analytical and strategic foundations of market segmentation. You will learn that segmentation is not a classification exercise — it is a resource allocation decision. The firm that segments well knows which customers to pursue, which to ignore, and how to build a value proposition that survives competitive pressure.

The module is analytically demanding in a specific way: you already know how to calculate NMC from Module 2. The challenge here is using NMC across multiple segments simultaneously to make a targeting recommendation under resource constraints. This is a portfolio problem, not a computation problem — and most students will not see that distinction until it is made explicit.

The central intellectual challenge is the targeting trap: the instinct to pursue the largest segment or the highest-NMC segment. The module's case is designed so that the "obvious" target is the wrong answer.

⚠ Prerequisite Check — M02 NMC Fluency Required

This module assumes you can calculate Net Marketing Contribution (NMC) fluently from Module 2. If you are uncertain, review the M02 App 3 worksheets before starting App 3 in this module. NMC is a tool here, not the subject — we are using it to compare segments and make targeting decisions.

Learning Objectives
LO1
Analysis
Explain why needs-first sequencing produces more actionable segments than demographic-first approaches; identify conditions under which demographic-first segmentation fails.
LO2
Application
Apply the 7-step needs-based segmentation process: group by need, identify segment characteristics, and construct a complete segment profile.
LO3
Evaluation
Assess segment attractiveness by scoring market demand, competitive intensity, and market access; explain why a firm with high share in an unattractive segment should consider exiting it.
LO4
Application / Analysis
Calculate NMC, Marketing ROS, and Marketing ROI across segments; identify which segment combination maximizes total return under a resource constraint.
LO5
Analysis
Distinguish among mass-market, large-segment, adjacent, multi-segment, niche, and subsegment targeting strategies; select the right strategy given resource level and competitive position.
LO6
Synthesis
Construct a complete 4-element positioning statement (target market, competitive set, value proposition, reasons to believe); evaluate statements for completeness and strategic alignment.

What the Chapter Covers Beyond the Slide Deck

Gate 1 tests these topics. They do not appear on the slides. You must find them in Chapter 5 before taking Gate 1.

  • 1
    The Demographic Trap
    pp. 163–164 — Why segmenting first on demographics can produce up to 729 meaningless combinations and why starting with needs avoids this.
  • 2
    Health Insurance Pivot Case
    Fig. 5-11, pp. 167–168 — A firm held 40% share in its least-valuable segment (revenue index 100) while Premium Buyers had index 181. What this means strategically.
  • 3
    CRM Program Types
    pp. 181–184 — Mass personalization vs. mass customization vs. customer relationship management. What triggers each level and when CRM is justified.
  • 4
    Customer Touch Points
    pp. 186–187 — Direct vs. indirect touch points; why indirect touch points (news, word-of-mouth) are sometimes more powerful in acquiring new customers.
  • 5
    B2B Segmentation Forces
    Fig. 5-6, pp. 161–162 — Firm demographics, company culture, and usage behaviors as B2B parallels to consumer segmentation forces. Growth-oriented entrepreneurs vs. cost-focused sustainers.

Before You Begin — Study Tips
  • Read Chapter 5 actively. The slide deck and lecture video deliberately leave out five topics that Gate 1 tests. The chapter covers more than this module's slides — that is intentional. Find the five chapter-exclusive topics listed above before you take Gate 1.
  • Complete the apps before the case. App 3 (Segment Portfolio Optimizer) is a direct preview of the Apex Athletic case structure. Students who run the Silicon Sealants scenario in App 3 before opening the case arrive knowing what questions to ask.
  • Post the discussion before starting the case. The discussion argument you write will become the VP Sales rebuttal you need in Case Part 4. The sequence is intentional.
  • Show your calculation steps. The case rubric awards partial credit only when intermediate steps are visible — Revenue, Gross Profit, M&S Expense, NMC, then ROI. A final number without a work trail earns reduced credit regardless of accuracy.
  • The biggest market is not always the best target. One segment in the Apex Athletic case will appear attractive by size alone. It is not. Your attractiveness analysis and NMC calculations will reveal why.
