Market Segmentation and Segmentation Strategies

Segmentation is not a labeling exercise. It is a resource allocation decision: who gets the offer, who gets the budget, and who the firm intentionally walks away from.

Part II · Market Analysis 195 points Two-Gate Accountability
Central Question
What this module asks you to figure out
Who do you serve - and who do you walk away from?

Module 05 turns segmentation into a strategic choice about resource allocation, not just a descriptive exercise. You will learn how needs-based segments are formed, how to score attractiveness, how to measure segment profitability, and how to choose a targeting strategy that matches your firm's resources and competitive position.

i
Why this matters

Segmentation is not about making the market look neat. It is about deciding where a firm should spend attention and capital, and where it should deliberately stop spending.

Learning Objectives
LO1
Knowledge / Comprehension
Explain why segmentation begins with needs rather than demographics, and distinguish between core needs and differentiating benefits in a market segment.
LO2
Application
Build a needs-based segmentation map from customer characteristics and identify which benefit differentiates each segment.
LO3
Evaluation
Assess segment attractiveness by scoring market demand, competitive intensity, and market access; explain why a firm with high share in an unattractive segment should consider exiting it.
LO4
Application / Analysis
Calculate NMC, Marketing ROS, and Marketing ROI across segments; identify which segment combination maximizes total return under a resource constraint.
LO5
Analysis
Distinguish among mass-market, large-segment, adjacent, multi-segment, niche, and subsegment targeting strategies; select the right strategy given resource level and competitive position.
LO6
Synthesis
Construct a complete 4-element positioning statement (target market, competitive set, value proposition, reasons to believe); evaluate statements for completeness and strategic alignment.

Two Traps to Watch For

This module contains two recurring errors that students make when they move too quickly from segmentation theory to strategy. Both traps are visible in the chapter material and are reinforced by the apps and case.

Starting with demographics instead of needs

The demographic trap creates lots of tidy-looking buckets that are not actually useful. Chapter 5 shows why starting with demographics can produce up to 729 combinations without telling you what customers really want. Start with needs, then work outward to the benefit that differentiates each segment.

Where to find it: Chapter 5 pp. 163-164; Toothpaste needs-based segment example.

Treating size, share, or attractiveness as the same thing

Segment size is only one input. A large segment with many competitors can score below a smaller fast-growing segment, and a firm can hold high share in a segment that should still be exited. Market access is the veto factor, and profitability can overturn a simple volume story.

Where to find it: Segment attractiveness framework, Health Insurance Pivot case, Glacier Ridge profitability example.
