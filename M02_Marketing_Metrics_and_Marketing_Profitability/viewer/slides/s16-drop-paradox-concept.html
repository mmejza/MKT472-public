The Drop Paradox: Why Net Profit Is the Wrong Metric
A product line with negative net profit can make the company worse off if eliminated
The Trap — Using Net Profit
Management looks at a product line with a net loss of −$0.7M. Conclusion: "It's losing money. Drop it."
After the drop, the company's overall profit falls. Management is confused — the line was losing money, but eliminating it made things worse.
Net profit includes allocated fixed overhead that does NOT leave with the line.
The Fix — Using NMC
The same product line has NMC = +$1.2M. It generates $1.2M more in gross profit than it requires in marketing expense.
That $1.2M is covering a portion of the company's fixed overhead. Eliminating the line removes the NMC — but the fixed overhead stays, now unfunded.
Drop rule: a positive-NMC line must be retained, regardless of net profit sign.
The Mechanism
Before drop:
Company NMC = $8.0M
Fixed overhead = $7.0M
Net income = $1.0M
After dropping +NMC line:
NMC falls by $1.2M → $6.8M
Fixed overhead still = $7.0M
Net income = −$0.2M
Result:
Line appeared to cost $0.7M in net profit. Dropping it cost $1.2M in NMC — a net harm of $1.2M, not a savings.
