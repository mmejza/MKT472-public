Reading Strategy
📝
Write exact figures

Several Gate 1 questions require a specific number or percentage — not a range or approximation. When you see a data point in a figure or footnote, write it down exactly as it appears.

📋
Record complete lists

Chapter 2 contains one important four-item list. Gate 1 will ask you to identify or apply individual items from that list. Read each term carefully — common business words not on the list will be offered as distractors.

🔢
Work the comparison tables

Gate 2 numeric questions pull directly from Figures 2-1, 2-4, and 2-15. As you read each comparative table, calculate the year-over-year differences yourself — don't just read them passively.

⚠️
Trust the math over your intuition

Three questions in this guide are flagged counterintuitive. In each case, the chapter shows a result that contradicts what most people would predict. Verify the arithmetic, then accept the result — Gate 1 and the case both test whether you caught it.

Focus Questions
Gate 1 · Chapter Only — Answer these five from the chapter before opening Gate 1
1
Gate 1 · Chapter Only 📍 pp. 42–44 · Figure 2-4 ⚠️ Counterintuitive
BioTronics tripled its pre-tax profits over three years while simultaneously losing market share. According to Figure 2-4, approximately how much additional pre-tax profit would BioTronics have earned over those three years had it maintained its base-year market share of 10 percent?
Record the exact dollar amount from the "Lost Profits" row in Figure 2-4, then note which single year produced the largest gap. The total and the year-3 figure are both tested.
2
Gate 1 · Chapter Only 📍 p. 44
Chapter 2 states that marketing performance metrics must satisfy four specific criteria to be useful. What are all four criteria, stated in the exact terms the chapter uses?
Write down each term exactly as it appears — all four in order. Gate 1 will ask you to identify which criterion is described by a scenario, or identify which word is NOT on this list. Plausible-sounding substitutes will be offered as distractors.
3
Gate 1 · Chapter Only 📍 p. 44
A CEO survey cited in Chapter 2 revealed that a majority of chief executives believe marketing lacks meaningful performance metrics. What exact percentage held that view, and which organization conducted the survey?
Record both the percentage (not a range — an exact number) and the full organization name. Both are tested independently. This statistic is the chapter's explanation for why Net Marketing Contribution was developed as a credibility tool.
4
Gate 1 · Chapter Only 📍 pp. 59–60 · Figures 2-15 & 2-18 ⚠️ Counterintuitive
Figure 2-15 organizes Santa Fe Sportswear's performance by product line, and Figure 2-18 reorganizes the same data by customer segment. Both produce identical company-level totals for NMC, sales, and profit. What does the customer-segment view reveal that the product-line view conceals — and why do the two frameworks produce the same aggregate numbers?
Note the three customer segment names used in Figure 2-18 (not the five product line names). Then identify: how many segments are profitable in the customer view vs. how many product lines appear unprofitable in the product view. This is the insight Gate 1 will test.
5
Gate 1 · Chapter Only 📍 pp. 54–56 ⚠️ Counterintuitive
Santa Fe Sportswear's casual shorts product line reports a net profit of negative $1 million. Chapter 2 calculates what happens to the company's overall profit if this line is dropped, and separately what happens if both unprofitable lines are dropped. What are the two resulting company profit figures, and why does eliminating a money-losing line make the company worse off?
Write down the company profit before the drop ($10M), after dropping casual shorts alone, and after dropping both casual shorts and knitted sweaters. Then record the one metric — not net profit — that the chapter says should drive the drop decision.
Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides — Answer these three; you will also need the slide deck to complete Gate 2 fully
6
Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides 📍 pp. 50–53 · Figure 2-12
Chapter 2 introduces a rule of thumb for estimating marketing and sales expenses when they are not separately reported. Based on the data in Figure 2-12, what percentage of a company's SGA expenses should be used to estimate MSE — and if a company reports gross profit of $48.5 million and SGA of $23.5 million, what is its estimated Net Marketing Contribution?
Gate 2 Q1 is a numeric entry question where SGA is given but not pre-separated. Practice the two-step: (1) multiply SGA by the percentage to get estimated MSE, then (2) subtract MSE from gross profit to get NMC. Confirm: $48.5M − ($23.5M × 75%) = ?
7
Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides 📍 pp. 54–56 ⚠️ Counterintuitive
Given the following product-line data — Total company NMC = $4.95M; one line has NMC = +$0.22M but net profit = −$0.18M — calculate the new total company NMC if that line is dropped. What does your answer reveal about the correct metric for a drop decision?
Gate 2 Q7 is a numeric entry question. The calculation is: new total NMC = current total NMC − dropped line's NMC. The allocated fixed overhead does not disappear when the line is dropped. Your answer should be less than $4.95M — record it to two decimal places.
8
Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides 📍 pp. 41–44 · Figure 2-3
BioTronics operated in a market growing at roughly 21 percent annually, yet grew its own sales at only 15 percent per year. Using the compounding formula Market(t) = Current × (1 + g)ᵗ: if a company starts with 10 percent share in a $100M market growing at 20 percent annually, how much revenue must it earn in Year 3 to maintain that share?
Gate 2 Q4 is a numeric entry question. Step 1: compute market size in Year 3 using the formula. Step 2: multiply by the share percentage. The answer is the company's required Year 3 revenue. Practice this with the BioTronics figures from Figure 2-3 before Gate 2 opens.
⭐ Highest-Probability Gate 1 Targets
  • The $41 million forfeited profit figure — Chapter 2, Figure 2-4. Gate 1 will ask for the cumulative three-year total and may ask which year produced the largest single-year gap. Both require reading Figure 2-4, not the text summary around it.
  • The four metric criteria in exact order — Chapter 2, p. 44. Gate 1 will offer a plausible fifth term (a common business word not on the list) as a distractor. The only reliable defense is writing down all four terms while reading.
  • The 51 percent CEO statistic and its source organization — Chapter 2, p. 44. Gate 1 will offer adjacent percentages (31%, 41%, 61%, 71%) and plausible organization names as distractors. Both the number and the organization name are required.
These targets are not hinted at in the slides. If you are pressed for time, read pp. 42–44 and p. 44 first, record all three sets of data, then read the rest of the chapter.
