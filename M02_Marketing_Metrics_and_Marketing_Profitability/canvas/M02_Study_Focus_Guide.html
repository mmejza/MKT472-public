Reading Strategy for Chapter 2
📝
Write exact figures as you go
Several Gate 1 targets depend on an exact number or percentage, not a range. When Chapter 2 gives a dollar figure, percentage, or survey statistic, record it exactly as shown in the figure or text.
📋
Record the full four-item list
One Gate 1 question depends on the chapter's exact four criteria for a valid metric. Write all four terms down in the chapter's wording so distractor terms do not mislead you later.
🔢
Work the comparison tables yourself
Figures 2-4, 2-12, and 2-15 matter because they require reading across rows, not just scanning totals. Recreate the intermediate steps while you read so the Gate 2 numeric entries feel procedural instead of new.
⚠️
Trust the accounting logic, not instinct
Chapter 2 includes counterintuitive results about lost profit, line drops, and customer-segment accounting. Pause when a result feels wrong and follow the math, because that tension is exactly what the assessments test.

BioTronics Lost-Profit Figure
Gate 1 · Chapter Only ⚠️ Counterintuitive
pp. 42–44 · Fig. 2-4
BioTronics tripled its pre-tax profits over three years while simultaneously losing market share. According to Figure 2-4, approximately how much additional pre-tax profit would BioTronics have earned over those three years had it maintained its base-year market share of 10 percent?
Record the exact cumulative lost-profit figure from Figure 2-4 and note which year produced the largest single-year gap. Gate 1 uses both the total and the chapter's framing of why growth alone concealed the problem.

Four Criteria for a Valid Metric
Gate 1 · Chapter Only

CEO Credibility Gap Statistic

A CEO survey cited in Chapter 2 revealed that a majority of chief executives believe marketing lacks meaningful performance metrics. What exact percentage held that view, and which organization conducted the survey?
Record both the exact percentage and the full organization name. Gate 1 may separate those into different distractor-heavy choices.
4
Customer-Segment vs. Product-Line View
Gate 1 · Chapter Only ⚠️ Counterintuitive
pp. 59–60 · Figs. 2-15 and 2-18
Figure 2-15 organizes Santa Fe Sportswear's performance by product line, and Figure 2-18 reorganizes the same data by customer segment. Both produce identical company-level totals for NMC, sales, and profit. What does the customer-segment view reveal that the product-line view conceals, and why do the two frameworks still produce the same aggregate numbers?
Record the three customer segment names and compare how many units look healthy in the segment view versus the product-line view. The chapter is testing whether you understand what reclassification changes and what it does not.
5
Product-Line Drop Calculation
Gate 1 · Chapter Only ⚠️ Counterintuitive
pp. 54–56
Santa Fe Sportswear's casual shorts product line reports a net profit of negative $1 million. Chapter 2 calculates what happens to the company's overall profit if this line is dropped, and separately what happens if both unprofitable lines are dropped. What are the two resulting company profit figures, and why does eliminating a money-losing line make the company worse off?
Write down the company profit before the drop, after dropping casual shorts alone, and after dropping both weak lines. Then record which metric the chapter says should drive the decision instead of net profit.

6
Estimating NMC from SGA
Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides
pp. 50–53 · Fig. 2-12
Chapter 2 introduces a rule of thumb for estimating marketing and sales expenses when they are not separately reported. Based on the data in Figure 2-12, what percentage of a company's SGA expenses should be used to estimate MSE, and if a company reports gross profit of $48.5 million and SGA of $23.5 million, what is its estimated Net Marketing Contribution?
Practice the two-step process: estimate MSE from SGA, then subtract that estimate from gross profit to get NMC. Gate 2 changes the numbers but preserves that exact sequence.
7
Drop Decision Using NMC
Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides ⚠️ Counterintuitive
pp. 54–56
Given the following product-line data, total company NMC equals $4.95 million and one line has NMC of +$0.22 million but net profit of −$0.18 million. Calculate the new total company NMC if that line is dropped. What does your answer reveal about the correct metric for a drop decision?
Use the chapter's logic, not intuition: when a positive-NMC line is dropped, total company NMC must fall. The fixed overhead logic from the reading is the key reason why.
8
Market Compounding and Share Maintenance
Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides
pp. 41–44 · Fig. 2-3
BioTronics operated in a market growing at roughly 21 percent annually, yet grew its own sales at only 15 percent per year. Using the compounding formula Market(t) = Current × (1 + g)^t, if a company starts with 10 percent share in a $100 million market growing at 20 percent annually, how much revenue must it earn in Year 3 to maintain that share?
Complete the calculation in two stages: first compute Year 3 market size, then apply the 10 percent share. The reading and slides both reinforce that the required revenue rises with market growth even when share is unchanged.
⚠️
High-Risk Reading Traps
Three Chapter 2 ideas routinely cause errors unless you stop and write out the logic:
  • Growth can hide decline: BioTronics improved profit and still destroyed value because the market was compounding faster than the firm.
  • Net profit is not the drop criterion: a line can show negative net profit and still contribute positive NMC, so removing it can lower total company profitability.
  • Reclassification changes the story, not the totals: product-line and customer-segment views can produce identical company aggregates while pointing to very different strategic problems.
