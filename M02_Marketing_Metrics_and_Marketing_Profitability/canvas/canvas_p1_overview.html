Two Questions This Module Answers
Question One

Can a company triple its profits over three years and simultaneously destroy $41 million in value it never knew it was losing?

This is the BioTronics paradox. You will need to read the chapter to find the number — it is not in the slides.

Question Two

Should you eliminate a product line that is losing $180,000 per year — or would dropping it actually make the company worse off?

The answer depends entirely on one number. App 2 will show you what that number is and why net profit is the wrong metric to look at.

Why two gates? A single gate before the lecture creates one gap: students who score just above 70% proceed without genuine chapter internalization. The two-gate model closes this. Students who game Gate 1 cannot game Gate 2 — the applied numeric questions require both sources.

Five Things Only in the Chapter

The slide deck and lecture video do not cover everything. These five topics appear only in Chapter 2. Gate 1 will test whether you found them. Use the page references below to locate them while reading.

  • 1
    The $41 million lost profit figure. BioTronics forfeited this much in pre-tax profit over three years by failing to maintain market share in a growing market. The chapter shows exactly how to calculate it.
    pp. 42–44, Fig. 2-4
  • 2
    The CEO credibility gap statistic. The Conference Board survey found that over half of Fortune 500 CEOs believe marketing lacks meaningful performance metrics. This statistic explains why NMC was invented.
    p. 44
  • 3
    Four criteria for a valid marketing metric. The chapter names four specific properties every marketing metric must have. You will need to know all four by name.
    p. 44
  • 4
    Customer-segment vs. product-line NMC accounting. Two frameworks for calculating NMC produce identical company totals but completely different strategic insights. The customer-segment framework is chapter-only.
    pp. 59–60, Fig. 2-18
  • 5
    The product-line drop calculation. The chapter shows what happens to total company profit when you eliminate a line with negative net profit. The quantitative result — and why it is counterintuitive — is not in the slides.
    pp. 54–56
