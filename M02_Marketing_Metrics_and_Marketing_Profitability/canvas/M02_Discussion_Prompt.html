Background

Cascadia ActiveWear is a mid-size regional apparel company with $54 million in annual revenue. Its Year 3 performance data has just been reviewed at a senior leadership meeting. The marketing team has been working with the company's three interactive performance apps, and the results are concerning:

Metric Cascadia's Result Context
Sales Revenue $54.0M Growing 11% annually
Gross Profit $22.14M (41%) Margin stable
SGA Expenses (undivided) $16.80M Apply 75% rule → MSE = $12.60M
Net Marketing Contribution $9.54M  
Marketing ROS 17.7% F500 average: 23.5% — below average
Marketing ROI 75.7% Below 100% floor — program does not recover its own cost
Product Line: Outdoor Accessories
Net Marketing Contribution +$0.30M NMC is positive — line contributes more GP than it costs in marketing
Allocated Fixed Overhead $0.48M Company-level fixed costs assigned to this line
Net Profit (before tax) −$0.18M Negative because allocated overhead exceeds NMC

The Leadership Debate

After reviewing the data, the Vice President of Finance and the Vice President of Marketing have reached sharply different conclusions about what to do. Both are speaking from the same data set.

VP of Finance Three proposals

"The data is unambiguous. Our Marketing ROI is 75.7% — we are not recovering our marketing investment. We are paying $12.60 million in marketing and sales expenses to generate only $9.54 million in NMC. That is the definition of an inefficient marketing program.

My recommendation has three parts:

1. Reduce marketing and sales expenses by 20%.

If MROI is below 100%, the solution is to spend less until the ratio recovers.

2. Drop the Outdoor Accessories product line.

It loses $180,000 per year. Eliminating it reduces our exposure to an underperforming product category.

3. Cut core product prices by 10% to grow market share.

More volume at a lower price will increase total NMC by driving more customers into the calculation.

All three of these actions address the MROI problem directly."

VP of Marketing Counter-argument

"I agree that MROI of 75.7% is a serious problem. But all three of the Finance VP's proposed solutions will make our NMC worse, not better.

On the price cut:

A 10% price reduction compresses our gross margin significantly. I ran this scenario in App 1. The additional volume does not compensate for the margin collapse — NMC falls, not rises.

On the Outdoor Accessories line:

The Finance VP is looking at net profit, not NMC. The line generates +$0.30M in NMC — it covers its own marketing costs and contributes to overhead. Drop it, and our total NMC falls by exactly $0.30M. The company is $300,000 worse off.

The MROI problem:

It is real, but it requires fixing the gross margin structure — not cutting the programs that are actually generating positive marketing profit."
