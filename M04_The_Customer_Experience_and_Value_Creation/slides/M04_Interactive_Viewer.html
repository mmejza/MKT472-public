LO1–LO3 Economic Value
Economic Value = Competitor Lifecycle Cost − Our Lifecycle Cost
A higher-priced product can deliver economic value when its total lifecycle costs are lower than the competitor's.

LO3 — Why willingness-to-pay ≠ economic value: Segments differ in risk aversion, time preference, switching costs, budget constraints, and trust in cost claims. EV sets the pricing ceiling — market research determines where to actually price.

Three Ways to Create More Economic Value (LO2)

1. Reduce Purchase Price ▼
Price the product below the competitor's price while maintaining the same total lifecycle cost structure. Creates immediate visible EV. Example: pricing a switch at $250 instead of $300 while LC costs remain equal.
2. Reduce Acquisition, Usage, or Ownership Costs ▼
Redesign the product or service model to reduce what customers spend beyond the purchase price. Examples: AirCap packaging reduces labor costs $0.70/shipment, offsetting its $0.25 higher price. SW Airlines includes baggage in ticket price.
3. Reduce Maintenance or Disposal Costs ▼
Engineering the product to require less service, last longer, or have salvage value at end of life. Example: longer-lasting filters reduce replacement frequency; products with recyclable components have lower disposal cost or positive residual value.

LO5 Perceived Value — Importance-Performance Analysis
Performance Index = 100 + Σ(Relative Advantage scores)
Cost of Purchase Index = 100 + Σ(Relative Cost Importance scores)
Perceived Value Index = Performance Index − Cost of Purchase Index

Award Rules for Relative Advantage

Company vs Competitor Rating DifferenceRelative Importance Points Awarded
Company higher by MORE than 1 point (≥ +2)Company earns full importance points for that attribute
Within ±1 point (tied or close)0 points — no perceived advantage
Company lower by MORE than 1 point (≤ −2)Company loses full importance points for that attribute

LO6 Emotional Value & Brand Personality
Emotional Value occurs when a brand's meaning resonates with the psychological needs, personalities, or personal values of buyers — creating positive affective responses (moods, emotions, attitudes).

Measuring Brand Personality

How brand personality is measured ▼
Researchers develop a list of human trait words (e.g., "honest," "daring," "reliable," "rugged") and ask consumers to rate how well each word describes a specific brand on a scale of 0–10 or using agree/disagree ratings. The set of traits receiving the highest ratings characterizes the brand's personality type. For example, if consumers rate Lexus highest on "upper class," "charming," and "sophisticated," the brand's personality is classified as Sophistication.
Why brand personality matters for value creation ▼
When two or more brands equally meet a consumer's rational needs (price, product, service benefits), the brand personality that most closely matches the buyer's psychological needs will be preferred. A brand that resonates emotionally can command a price premium, build stronger loyalty, and generate word-of-mouth advocacy that functional benefits alone cannot. Nike's association with achievement and excitement explains why consumers pay premium prices for what are functionally athletic shoes.
Challenges of emotional value ▼
Unlike economic value (measured in dollars) or relative performance value (measured in ratings), emotional value is difficult to quantify. It varies by individual psychology, culture, and life stage. It must be reinforced consistently across all brand touchpoints — advertising, product design, packaging, customer service, and social media. A single negative high-profile incident can undermine emotional value built over years.
Key principle: Products have personalities just as people do. The more carefully a brand's personality aligns with the psychological needs of its target segment, the greater the emotional value — and the stronger the competitive advantage when rational product benefits are comparable.
