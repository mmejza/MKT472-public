Background

Apex Industrial has just completed a perceived value analysis of its FilterPro X7 commercial filtration system. The analysis produced the following results:

Index Value What It Means
Overall Performance Index 128 FilterPro X7 delivers 28% more perceived benefit than the competitive average
Cost of Purchase Index 112 Customers perceive FilterPro X7 as 12% more expensive than the competitive average
PCV Index 16 128 − 112 = 16 → Superior value zone (positive PCV)

Two executives have reviewed this data and reached opposite conclusions about what Apex should do.

The Disagreement

Product Manager Arguing for a price increase

"We are 28% better than competitors on benefits and only 12% worse on cost. The net is positive — our PCV Index is 16, which puts us in the superior value zone. Companies in the superior value zone earn above-average profitability. If we raise our price, we capture more of the value we are already delivering. The data supports it."

Pricing Analyst Arguing against a price increase

"Our Cost of Purchase Index is already 112. That means customers already think we charge more than competitors. Raising price moves us deeper into cost-disadvantage territory. Yes, our performance is strong — but customers evaluate both sides of the equation. A higher price makes our cost perception worse and could push us closer to the inferior value zone. I recommend holding price or improving our cost perception first."

Your Task

  1. Take a clear position — side with either the Product Manager or the Pricing Analyst. State your position unambiguously in your first paragraph.
  2. Defend your position in 150–200 words using specific data and concepts from this module. Your post must cite at least two of the following: PCV Index value, value map zone classification, Fig 4-26 profitability logic, economic value, lifecycle cost comparison, indifference price logic.
  3. Devil's advocate (100–150 words). Now make the strongest case for the position you just argued against. Use specific data from this module to support the opposing argument. What is the best evidence the other side has? Where is your own argument most vulnerable?
Required Elements

Your initial post must include:

  • An unambiguous position statement in the first paragraph
  • At least two specific values from the module (e.g., PCV Index = 16, OPI = 128, CPI = 112)
  • A connection to Fig 4-26 profitability logic or the value map zone
  • A Part 2 devil's advocate section (100–150 words) making the strongest possible case against your own position with module-specific data

What a Strong Post Looks Like

The best posts will do all three of the following:

  1. Cite the PCV Index of 16 and connect it to the Fig 4-26 profitability argument — not just name it.
  2. Engage the tension between the positive PCV (16) and the above-100 Cost of Purchase Index (112) — both signals are real; a strong post explains which one should drive the pricing decision and why.
  3. Use at least one calculation to support the position — for example, showing what happens to the PCV Index if the price increases and the CPI rises, or calculating the margin between the current PCV and zero.
Connection to the Case Analysis

The Apex Industrial case analysis (Part 4) asks you to make a specific pricing recommendation grounded in calculations from Parts 1–3. This discussion seeds the argument you will refine in that recommendation.

Both scenarios place Apex in the superior value zone — the discussion builds the conceptual argument; the case provides the exact numbers.
