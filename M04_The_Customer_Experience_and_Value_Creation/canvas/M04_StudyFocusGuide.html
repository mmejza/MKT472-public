Reading Strategy for Chapter 4
🗂️
Build the company map
Chapter 4 uses a named company example for each lifecycle cost category. Create a two-column table as you read: cost category on the left, company name and what they did on the right. Gate 1 Q2 tests this mapping.
⚠️
Watch for the sign inversion
The Cost of Purchase Index uses the same method as the performance indexes — but the sign means the opposite. A positive relative advantage score on a cost component means your product costs more, not less. Mark this passage when you encounter it.
📊
Work the figures yourself
Figures 4-20 through 4-24 show a complete perceived value calculation chain. Don't just read the results — cover the RA column and calculate each number yourself. Gate 2 Q5 and Q6 test this skill with different inputs.
🏷️
Memorize the five types
Brand personality has five named types with specific traits. Gate 1 Q5 gives you a brand scenario you have never seen and asks you to classify it. If you know the types and traits cold, classification becomes quick. Flashcards help here.
Eight Focus Questions — Answer These While You Read
1
Total Customer Experience — Three-Stage Model
Gate 1 · Chapter Only
Fig 4-2 · pp. 116–117
What are the three stages of the Total Customer Experience model, and what specific activities or interactions occur in each stage? Which stage does the chapter say most product-focused firms neglect — and what strategic opportunity does that neglected stage create for firms willing to invest in understanding it?
Record all three stage names and 2–3 activities per stage from Figure 4-2. Flag the neglected stage and the chapter's explanation of what happens there.
2
Lifecycle Cost Categories — Six Types with Industry Examples
Gate 1 · Chapter Only
pp. 126–132 · Figs 4-11 to 4-15
Chapter 4 identifies six lifecycle cost categories and uses a specific named company to illustrate each one. For each of the six categories, what is the category name, what does it include, and which company (and product) does the chapter use as its example?
Build a 3-column table: category name / what it includes / company example. Key names to look for: Sealed Air AirCap, Loctite Quick Metal, Kathon, American Hospital Supply, GE Capital. The sixth uses a packaging cost scenario.
3
Weyerhaeuser Particleboard — Economic Value Discovery
Gate 1 · Chapter Only
pp. 118–119, 127
How did Weyerhaeuser discover that its particleboard created economic value for furniture makers — even though it was priced higher than competitors? What specific method did Weyerhaeuser use to uncover the customer problem, what two production problems did it find, and which lifecycle cost category did each problem belong to?
Record the discovery method (it involved visiting customers, not surveying them), both production problems with their cost category labels, and how the new product addressed them while still being more expensive to purchase.
4
The 8%/80% Perception Gap
Gate 1 · Chapter Only ⚠️ Counterintuitive
p. 115
Chapter 4 opens with a statistic about the gap between how companies assess their customer experience and how customers actually rate it. What are the two specific percentages? More importantly: does the chapter argue this gap is primarily an execution failure (companies are trying but not succeeding) or a measurement failure (companies don't even know what customers experience) — and what evidence does it give?
The answer is counterintuitive: most people assume execution failure, but the chapter argues measurement failure. Record both percentages and the chapter's diagnosis — this exact distinction is what Gate 1 tests.
5
Brand Personality — Five Types and Defining Traits
Gate 1 · Chapter Only
Fig 4-27 · pp. 143–144
What are the five brand personality types identified in Chapter 4? For each type, what are the defining personality traits, and which specific brand does the chapter associate with it? After you complete the list, try classifying one brand you know well that the chapter does not mention — which type fits it and which traits apply?
Record all five types with their full trait lists from Figure 4-27. Gate 1 Q5 presents a brand scenario (not one of the chapter's named examples) and asks you to classify it — practice classification now.
Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides Synthesis
6
Economic Value Formula and Indifference Price
Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides
Fig 4-10 · pp. 126–131
Using Figure 4-10 (the telecommunications switch comparison), what are the total lifecycle costs of each switch, and what is the economic value? The company's switch costs $75 more to buy — yet the chapter says it creates positive economic value. At what price would a rational buyer be exactly indifferent between the two switches, and how does that ceiling price follow from the lifecycle cost data?
Calculate: Competitor LCC, Company LCC, Economic Value = Competitor LCC − Company LCC. Derive the indifference price yourself: it equals the Competitor LCC minus the Company's non-price costs. Gate 2 Q1 and Q2 use different numbers — this exercise builds the method.
7
Cost of Purchase Index — Sign Inversion Logic
Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides ⚠️ Counterintuitive
Fig 4-24 · pp. 139–141
Figure 4-24 shows that the company's Purchase Price has a relative advantage score of +27. A common mistake is to read this as meaning the company is 27% better than competitors on price. Why is this wrong? What does a positive relative advantage score actually mean for a cost component — and how does the interpretation differ from the product performance index?
The answer is counterintuitive: in the Cost of Purchase Index, a higher rating means the product costs more — which is a competitive disadvantage. A positive RA score = the company charges MORE than competitors on that cost dimension. Record this rule precisely — Gate 2 Q6 and the case both test it.
8
Conjoint CVI Calculation and Strategy Profit Comparison
Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides
Figs 4-29, 4-30 · pp. 144–147
Using Figure 4-29 (preference values) and Figure 4-30 (the Silent Floor strategy comparison), calculate the Customer Value Index for Strategy A (40% labor savings, 10-year warranty, +40% price premium, no callbacks). Strategy A has a slightly lower CVI than Strategy B — yet the chapter says Strategy A is likely more profitable. What is the mechanism that explains how the strategy with the lower CVI generates more profit?
CVI = sum of preference values for each selected attribute level. The profit mechanism is in the price premium rows: compare +40% vs. +20% premiums. The CVI gap is entirely explained by the price attribute — B charges less, which buyers prefer, which also means less margin for the firm. Gate 2 Q8 uses different preference values and industry context.
🎯 Highest-Probability Gate 1 Targets
If you are short on time, prioritize these three areas — they appear on Gate 1 across all question variants and cannot be answered from the slides alone:
  • Company-to-category matching (Q2): Know which named company illustrates each of the six lifecycle cost categories. The scenario in Gate 1 will describe a cost situation; you match it to the category and the example.
  • 8%/80% gap interpretation (Q4): The chapter's argument — that this is a measurement failure, not an execution failure — is the exact construct Gate 1 tests. The distinction matters.
  • Brand personality classification (Q5): Gate 1 will give you a brand you have not seen before and ask which of the five personality types it belongs to. Know the full trait list for all five types, not just the named examples.
