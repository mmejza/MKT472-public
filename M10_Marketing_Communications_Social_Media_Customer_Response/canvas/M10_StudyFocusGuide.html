Chapter 10 Reading Targets

Answer these 8 questions while reading Chapter 10. They are drawn directly from Gate 1 and Gate 2 targets. This guide primes your reading — it does not give away gate answers.

How to use this guide: Read Chapter 10 with these questions open. When you encounter the relevant section, write a brief answer in your own words. Students who answer all 8 before Gate 1 consistently score above the 70% threshold on the first attempt. Students who skip this step frequently need their second attempt.
Do not skim. Gate 1 questions test five topics that appear only in the chapter — not in the slides or lecture. The AFLAC case, the trade promotion trap, the Loctite rebrand, the Post-it free-sample case, and the promotional sub-types (pp. 341–342, 358, 360, 362, 367–368) cannot be answered from slides alone. Budget the full 70–80 minutes for the reading.
Questions 1–5  ·  Gate 1 Targets  ·  Chapter-Only Content
Q1 Gate 1 p. 358

AFLAC duck campaign. Before the duck campaign launched, what was AFLAC's name recognition rating in the United States? What happened to revenues in the first two weeks after the commercials aired? And what eventually caused the campaign to lose effectiveness over time — what does Chapter 10 call this phenomenon?

Why this matters
This case illustrates both the power of message frequency (awareness → 90%) and its ceiling (wear-out). Gate 1 tests a specific statistic — look for the exact percentage.
Q2 Gate 1 pp. 367–368 · Fig. 10-24

Trade promotion forward-buying trap (Figure 10-24). When the supplier announced a price promotion in advance, what did the retailer do in the month before the promotion began, and why? Over the full three-month window (before, during, after), which party's gross profit declined relative to the no-promotion baseline — and by approximately what percentage?

Why this matters
This is the case's central counterintuitive result. The month-2 volume spike looks like success. The 3-month comparison reveals the loss. Make sure you understand both numbers.
Q3 Gate 1 p. 360

Loctite / RC-601 → Quick Metal. What was the original brand name? Who was the original target audience, and why did the initial ad campaign fail despite a technically superior product? Who turned out to be the actual buyers? What happened to sales after the relaunch?

Why this matters
This case illustrates the "ad copy and target audience" root cause of low CRI comprehension. The fix wasn't the product or the budget — it was the audience definition and the message.

Q5 Gate 1 p. 362

Post-it Note free-sample case. For how long did 3M's advertising campaign for Post-it Notes fail, and across how many test markets? What was 3M's corporate policy that initially blocked the solution? How did the marketing director circumvent it? What does this case imply about when advertising alone is insufficient to stimulate customer action?

Why this matters
This case extends the CRI framework: even perfect comprehension doesn't guarantee action if the product's benefits can only be discovered through use. Knowing the product ≠ wanting the product ≠ buying the product.
Questions 6–8  ·  Gate 2 Targets  ·  Chapter + Slides Synthesis
Q6 Gate 2 p. 355 · Fig. 10-15

Customer Response Index hierarchy. List the five stages in the CRI hierarchy in order. For the department-store campaign example in Figure 10-15, write out the CRI calculation step by step and confirm the 4.4% result. Then identify which stage had the lowest rate — and note whether that stage was also the one that would have yielded the highest marginal CRI gain if improved.

Formula
CRI = Exposure × Awareness × Comprehension × Intention × Purchase
Why this matters
Gate 2 Q1 gives you different stage inputs and asks you to compute the CRI. Gate 2 Q2 asks which stage improvement yields the highest marginal gain. Practice both on the chapter example before Gate 2.
Q7 Gate 2 pp. 359–360

GRP and weighted average message frequency. Write out the GRP formula and calculate it for the chapter example (60% reach, 4 exposures). Then work through the weighted-average message frequency table on p. 359: multiply each recall level by its percentage, sum the column, and confirm the ~3.85 result. What is the strategic implication of the gap between messages sent (8) and average messages received (~4)?

Formulas
GRP = Reach% × Frequency
WA Freq = Σ (Recall Level × % Recalling That Many)
Why this matters
Gate 2 Q3 gives you different reach and frequency inputs. Gate 2 Q4 gives you a different recall distribution. Both are straightforward if you've practiced the formulas on the chapter data.
Q8 Gate 2 pp. 364–366

Advertising carryover — two formulas. Write out the carryover model for Hart Schaffner Marx (p. 365): what is the current-period sales impact? What is the total multi-period impact using the total-impact formula? What percentage of the total does the current period represent? What does this imply about a manager who uses only current-period ROI to evaluate the ad budget?

Formulas
Current-Period Impact = Multiplier × Ad Spend
Total Impact = Current ÷ (1 − Carryover Coefficient)
Why this matters
Gate 2 Q6 and Q7 test these two formulas with different parameter values. The case Part 3 uses them with Apex Audio's inputs. If the formula is clear in your head from the chapter example, the case calculation is straightforward.
