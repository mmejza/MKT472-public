Reading Strategy for Chapter 10
📍
Mark the chapter-only cases
Flag the AFLAC duck campaign, the trade promotion trap, the Loctite rebrand, the Post-it sample case, and the promotional sub-types before you start. Gate 1 pulls from all five.
✍️
Write down the formulas
Next to each formula, write what it measures and which communication decision it supports so the logic stays visible when you revisit it.
⚠️
Do not trust the highest raw rate
CRI is a multiplicative diagnostic. The stage with the lowest rate is not always the stage that produces the biggest gain when improved, so read the marginal logic carefully.
🔗
Connect the metrics
Chapter 10 links CRI, GRP, weighted message frequency, and carryover into one decision sequence. Map that sequence now so Gate 2 feels like a review instead of a surprise.
Eight Focus Questions - Answer These While You Read
1
Gate 1 - Chapter Only
AFLAC duck campaign
Before the duck campaign launched, what was AFLAC's name recognition rating in the United States? What happened to revenues in the first two weeks after the commercials aired? And what eventually caused the campaign to lose effectiveness over time - what does Chapter 10 call this phenomenon?
Chapter 10 - p. 358
This case is the chapter's example of strong frequency effects and eventual wear-out. Gate 1 expects the exact statistic.
2
Gate 1 - Chapter Only
Trade promotion forward-buying trap
When the supplier announced a price promotion in advance, what did the retailer do in the month before the promotion began, and why? Over the full three-month window, which party's gross profit declined relative to the no-promotion baseline - and by approximately what percentage?
Chapter 10 - pp. 367-368 · Fig. 10-24
The month-2 spike looks like success, but the three-month comparison reveals the loss. Make sure you understand both views of the data.
3
Gate 1 - Chapter Only
Loctite / RC-601 to Quick Metal
What was the original brand name? Who was the original target audience, and why did the initial ad campaign fail despite a technically superior product? Who turned out to be the actual buyers? What happened to sales after the relaunch?
Chapter 10 - p. 360
This case shows that the real problem can be the message and the target, not the product or the budget.
4
Gate 1 - Chapter Only
Promotional sub-types
Chapter 10 identifies three sub-types of brand-action promotional communications. Define each one in a sentence and give a real example from the chapter: (a) Promotional Giveaway, (b) Promotional Gift, and (c) Minimum Purchase.
Chapter 10 - pp. 341-342
Gate 1 may give you a scenario and ask which sub-type it represents. Know the differences before you start.
5
Gate 1 - Chapter Only
Post-it Note free-sample case
For how long did 3M's advertising campaign for Post-it Notes fail, and across how many test markets? What was 3M's corporate policy that initially blocked the solution? How did the marketing director circumvent it? What does this case imply about when advertising alone is insufficient to stimulate customer action?
Chapter 10 - p. 362
This case extends the CRI framework: even perfect comprehension does not guarantee action if the product's benefits can only be discovered through use.
6
Gate 2 - Chapter + Slides
Customer Response Index hierarchy
List the five stages in the CRI hierarchy in order. For the department-store campaign example in Figure 10-15, write out the CRI calculation step by step and confirm the 4.4% result. Then identify which stage had the lowest rate - and note whether that stage was also the one that would have yielded the highest marginal CRI gain if improved.
Formula
CRI = Exposure × Awareness × Comprehension × Intention × Purchase
Chapter 10 - p. 355 · Fig. 10-15 · Slides S09-S12
Gate 2 uses a new set of stage inputs. Practice the chapter example first so the multiplication sequence feels automatic.
7
Gate 2 - Chapter + Slides
GRP and weighted average message frequency
Write out the GRP formula and calculate it for the chapter example (60% reach, 4 exposures). Then work through the weighted-average message frequency table on p. 359: multiply each recall level by its percentage, sum the column, and confirm the approximately 3.85 result. What is the strategic implication of the gap between messages sent (8) and average messages received (about 4)?
Formulas
GRP = Reach% × Frequency
WA Freq = Σ (Recall Level × % Recalling That Many)
Chapter 10 - pp. 359-360 · Slides S14-S16
Use the chapter numbers first, then connect them to the slide logic about media selection and actual frequency.
8
Gate 2 - Chapter + Slides
Advertising carryover - two formulas
Write out the carryover model for Hart Schaffner Marx (p. 365): what is the current-period sales impact? What is the total multi-period impact using the total-impact formula? What percentage of the total does the current period represent? What does this imply about a manager who uses only current-period ROI to evaluate the ad budget?
Formulas
Current-Period Impact = Multiplier × Ad Spend
Total Impact = Current ÷ (1 - Carryover Coefficient)
Chapter 10 - pp. 364-366 · Slides S22-S23
Gate 2 repeats these formulas with different parameters. If the chapter example is clear, the case calculation is straightforward.
