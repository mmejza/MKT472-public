Market Planning & Analysis

An analytically rigorous, textbook-driven, asynchronous online course covering the full arc of market-based strategy — from measuring customer value and market potential through competitive positioning, pricing, communications, and portfolio planning.

10 modules · 10 chapters ~5 hrs per module

What makes this course different

Marketing Is More Analytical Than Most People Realize

One of the goals of MKT 472 is to challenge the common misconception that marketing is primarily creative or promotional.

Effective marketing decisions depend on rigorous analysis. Throughout this course, you will learn how managers use customer data, market metrics, financial measures, forecasting tools, competitive assessments, and strategic frameworks to make better decisions.

Because this course emphasizes analysis rather than memorization, the textbook is essential. The slide viewer, lecture videos, apps, and cases are designed to reinforce and apply the chapter material--not replace it. Students who attempt to rely only on videos or slides typically struggle because many concepts, formulas, frameworks, and examples appear only in the textbook.

You will be asked to move beyond memorization and develop the ability to analyze situations, interpret evidence, evaluate alternatives, and justify recommendations. For many students, this is the first marketing course that combines quantitative analysis with strategic decision-making at this level.

The course is designed to be demanding, engaging, and highly practical. The skills developed here mirror the kinds of analytical thinking expected in professional marketing and business environments.

Bottom line: This is a textbook-driven course. The videos, slide viewer, apps, and case analyses assume you have already completed the assigned reading.

Part I — Market Orientation & Performance Metrics  (M01–M02)

M01 Market Orientation & Customer Lifetime Value Ch. 1 ▶

Establishes that firm profitability depends on how well a company satisfies and retains customers over time. The central challenge — the distributions-versus-averages problem — leads students to a counterintuitive insight: eliminating dissatisfied customers often reduces average profit rather than raising it.

  • LO1Trace the causal chain from insufficient customer focus through declining satisfaction to diminished shareholder value.
  • LO2Calculate and interpret a weighted-average Customer Satisfaction Index (CSI).
  • LO3Assess the financial case for investing in a complaint-resolution program.
  • LO4Distinguish customer retention from loyalty; classify customers; calculate a Customer Loyalty Index.
  • LO5Calculate customer life expectancy from a retention rate using N = 1 ÷ (1 − r).
  • LO6Compute CLV using the closed-form formula and the period-by-period present value method.
M02 Marketing Metrics & Marketing Profitability Ch. 2 ▶

Builds the financial vocabulary of marketing strategy. The central counterintuitive result: dropping a product line with negative net profit often reduces total company profit — a direct consequence of how net marketing contribution (NMC) isolates marketing's contribution from shared overhead.

  • LO1Distinguish financial (internal) performance metrics from marketing (external) performance metrics.
  • LO2Classify the four categories of marketing metrics and explain the forward/backward-looking distinction.
  • LO3Calculate net marketing contribution (NMC) and explain how it differs from net profit.
  • LO4Calculate marketing ROS and marketing ROI; benchmark against Fortune 500 averages.
  • LO5Use NMC decomposition to plan and evaluate alternative marketing strategies.
  • LO6Apply the market size growth formula and interpret NMC product/customer line management insights.

Part II — Market Analysis  (M03–M05)

M03 Market Potential, Market Demand & Market Share Ch. 3 ▶

Establishes the measurement vocabulary for sizing and tracking markets — how to calculate market potential, measure development stage, apply the product life cycle, and diagnose a company's share position relative to its potential.

  • LO1Distinguish generic, product category, and product form markets; construct examples of each.
  • LO2Define market potential and apply the formula to calculate unit and dollar potential.
  • LO3Calculate and interpret the Market Development Index (MDI).
  • LO4Identify the seven forces that prevent markets from reaching full potential and the adoption forces.
  • LO5Describe the four PLC stages and explain how demand, profit, pricing, and strategy differ across them.
  • LO6Calculate market share; construct and interpret a market share performance tree and MSI.
  • LO7Calculate SDI; use the MDI × SDI growth opportunity portfolio to classify growth strategies.
M04 Customer Value & the Customer Experience Ch. 4 ▶

Moves from market-level metrics to the individual customer — how companies quantify the value their products deliver across four distinct dimensions and use customer experience insights to drive product and service improvements.

  • LO1Define economic value and explain how lifecycle costs determine it (EV = Competitor LC Cost − Our LC Cost).
  • LO2Describe three ways a business can create more economic value.
  • LO3Explain why willingness-to-pay cannot be assumed from economic value alone.
  • LO4Calculate relative performance value using the fair price line and price-performance ratio.
  • LO5Calculate perceived value using importance-performance analysis and interpret the value map.
  • LO6Explain conjoint analysis (CVI) and emotional value / brand personality.
M05 Market Segmentation & Segmentation Strategies Ch. 5 ▶

Segmentation is not a labeling exercise. It is a resource allocation decision: who gets the offer, who gets the budget, and who the firm intentionally walks away from. The module turns that choice into a method by showing how needs-based segments are formed, how attractiveness is scored, and how targeting strategy must fit firm resources and competitive position.

  • LO1Explain why segmentation begins with needs rather than demographics, and distinguish between core needs and differentiating benefits in a market segment.
  • LO2Build a needs-based segmentation map from customer characteristics and identify which benefit differentiates each segment.
  • LO3Assess segment attractiveness by scoring market demand, competitive intensity, and market access; explain why a firm with high share in an unattractive segment should consider exiting it.
  • LO4Calculate NMC, Marketing ROS, and Marketing ROI across segments; identify which segment combination maximizes total return under a resource constraint.
  • LO5Distinguish among mass-market, large-segment, adjacent, multi-segment, niche, and subsegment targeting strategies; select the right strategy given resource level and competitive position.
  • LO6Construct a complete 4-element positioning statement (target market, competitive set, value proposition, reasons to believe); evaluate statements for completeness and strategic alignment.

Part III — Competitive Strategy  (M06–M08)

M06 Competitive Position & Sources of Advantage Ch. 6 ▶

Competitive position is treated as a measurable choice, not a list of options. The module compares cost, differentiation, and marketing advantage using explicit indices, then uses the Prisoner’s Dilemma and industry attractiveness to show why disciplined choice matters.

  • LO1Assess a firm's most defensible source of competitive advantage given its cost structure, product performance, and market position; explain why pursuing all three sources simultaneously undermines competitive clarity.
  • LO2Calculate a Variable Cost Advantage Index, a Differentiation Advantage score using importance-performance analysis, and a Relative Market Share index; interpret each metric's strategic implication.
  • LO3Distinguish the three sub-types of marketing advantage (market share, product line, channel); compute Relative Market Share; explain the mechanism linking market dominance to profitability.
  • LO4Analyze the Prisoner’s Dilemma payoff structure; identify the Nash equilibrium outcome; explain why mutual price-cutting destroys industry profit even though it is individually rational for each firm.
  • LO5Classify competitor behaviors as signals of financial distress or marketing leadership failure; distinguish competitive benchmarking (outside industry) from competitor benchmarking; describe the three-step benchmarking process.
  • LO6Score industry attractiveness across six Porter forces; compute an Industry Attractiveness Index; predict profit potential and recommend whether to enter, hold, or exit a competitive environment.
M07 Product Positioning, Branding & Product Line Strategies Ch. 7 ▶

Product positioning and brand strategy are linked through the customer value hierarchy, parity and differentiation choices, and brand equity scorecards. The module shows why superior products can still lose share and how product line decisions can either build or erode brand value.

  • LO1Trace the causal chain from weak product positioning through margin erosion, share loss, and declining brand equity; identify where differentiation investment interrupts that chain.
  • LO2Apply the Customer Value Hierarchy to classify product attributes as Core Benefit, Expected, Augmented, or Potential; distinguish Points of Parity from Points of Differentiation for a given competitive set.
  • LO3Calculate a Brand Assets Score and Brand Liabilities Score using the weighted scorecard (20% per dimension, 0–100 scale); compute Brand Equity and interpret the strategic implication of the assets-to-liabilities ratio.
  • LO4Classify brand growth strategies (line extension, brand extension, fighter brand, new brand) given a scenario; evaluate the conditions under which each strategy builds versus erodes brand equity.
  • LO5Assess a product line breadth decision by applying the ROA-by-lifecycle-stage data from Figure 7-24; identify when broad lines outperform narrow lines and when the advantage reverses at the Mature stage.
  • LO6Construct a repositioning recommendation that integrates differentiation source, brand encoding strategy, product line implications, and financial justification; ground the recommendation in case-specific data, not generic marketing language.
M08 Value-Based Pricing & Pricing Strategies Ch. 8 ▶

Pricing is a value capture decision, not a cost-plus exercise. The module uses economic value, performance value, and break-even analysis to test whether a price move actually improves profit under different market conditions.

  • LO1Explain why cost-based pricing often leads to underpricing or overpricing; identify when market-based pricing provides superior profit outcomes.
  • LO2Calculate economic value (value-in-use) by comparing total cost of ownership; determine the maximum justified premium over the competitor price.
  • LO3Compute performance-based value scores from conjoint weights; compare company versus competitor value across customer segments.
  • LO4Classify a market situation into the correct product life-cycle pricing strategy; justify why that strategy fits the market conditions.
  • LO5Apply the break-even percentage formula to price increases and decreases; interpret break-even market share versus break-even volume risk.
  • LO6Evaluate price direction under elasticity conditions and synthesize economic value, performance value, and break-even outputs into a recommendation.

Part IV — Go-to-Market Execution  (M09–M10)

M09 Marketing Channels & Channel Mapping Ch. 9 ▶

Channel structure determines what the firm actually receives after discounts, commissions, and transaction costs. The module compares direct, indirect, and mixed systems using pocket price, NMC, and ROI to show why the highest-margin channel can still be the wrong choice.

  • LO1Compute pocket price across a multi-tier channel by tracing the full waterfall of discounts, commissions, and transaction costs.
  • LO2Calculate NMC, Marketing ROS, and Marketing ROI; explain why the highest-margin channel can have the lowest ROI.
  • LO3Evaluate whether a channel strategy switch is justified by computing break-even market share and comparing it to current share.
  • LO4Distinguish direct, indirect, and mixed channel systems by cost structure, control, and profitability profile.
M10 Marketing Communications, Social Media & Customer Response Ch. 10 ▶

Communications performance has to be diagnosed before budget is reallocated. The module uses CRI, carryover, and three-month gross profit to separate weak response, underestimated advertising, and trade-promotion profit traps.

  • LO1Compute CRI from staged response rates and identify which stage improvement creates the largest marginal CRI gain.
  • LO2Calculate GRP and weighted average message frequency, then evaluate whether media intensity solves the diagnosed CRI bottleneck.
  • LO3Apply advertising carryover formulas to compare short-run ROI with total-impact ROI and interpret the strategic implications.
  • LO4Evaluate a trade promotion using three-month supplier gross profit and distinguish forward-buying from true demand growth.
