Reading Strategy for Chapter 3
📌
Mark what stays in the chapter
Gate 1 depends on chapter-only details the slides omit: Levitt's wording, Red Bull's market-share numbers, the seven market-development forces, and the IBM estimate. Flag those passages as you read.
🗂️
Build exact term lists
Chapter 3 includes sets that must be memorized precisely: three innovation types, seven constraints on market development, and the product life cycle stages tied to NMC. Write each list cleanly instead of relying on gist.
🧮
Practice the formulas by hand
Questions 6–8 are not recognition tasks. Work through market potential, MDI, MSI leakage, SPI, and SDI step by step so the Gate 2 numeric entries feel procedural rather than novel.
⚠️
Do not confuse related indexes
MDI, MSI, SPI, and SDI all look similar but answer different questions. As you read, write what each ratio measures and what managerial conclusion it supports so you do not mix them up later.
Eight Focus Questions — Answer These While You Read
1
Marketing Myopia
Gate 1 · Chapter Only
pp. 81–82
Theodore Levitt introduced the concept of marketing myopia to describe a common strategic failure. In your own words, what does marketing myopia mean, what causes it, and what is the primary consequence for a business that suffers from it?
Look for the chapter's emphasis on narrow definition, product-focused thinking, and missed market opportunity. Gate 1 tests examples of myopic behavior, not just the term itself.
2
Red Bull's Deliberate Narrow-Market Strategy
Gate 1 · Chapter Only
pp. 84–85
Chapter 3 uses Red Bull as an example of a deliberate narrow-market strategy rather than a blind spot. What is Red Bull's worldwide share of the energy drink market, what is its share of the broader worldwide nonalcoholic beverage market, and which major competitors entered the energy drink market with named brands?
Record the exact share figures and the competitor-brand pairings. The chapter uses these specifics to distinguish strategic focus from market blindness.
3
Three Innovation Types
Gate 1 · Chapter Only
pp. 88–89
Chapter 3 defines three distinct types of innovation and explains how each affects market potential differently. For each type, what is the name, how does the chapter define it, and which historical example does the chapter use to illustrate it?
Match the chapter's examples, including the ballpoint pen, microcomputer, and digital photography, to the correct innovation type. Gate 1 tests both definitions and examples.
4
Seven Forces Restricting Market Development
Gate 1 · Chapter Only
pp. 92–94
Chapter 3 names seven specific forces that prevent markets from reaching their full potential. What are all seven forces, and how does the chapter distinguish insufficient infrastructure from unavailability?
Write all seven by name, then tie the high-speed train and early-PC technical support examples to the right force. Gate 1 uses scenario matching here, not just list recall.
5
NMC Across the Product Life Cycle
Gate 1 · Chapter Only
pp. 97–98, 102
Chapter 3 traces how Net Marketing Contribution changes across each stage of the product life cycle. At what stage is NMC negative and why, at what stage does NMC peak, is that the same stage in which market demand peaks, and what did IBM estimate as the total market potential for computers in the 1950s?
Use Figure 3-10 and the IBM example together. The chapter is explicit that NMC peak and demand peak are not the same thing, and Gate 1 tests that distinction.

6
Market Potential Formula and MDI
Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides
pp. 86–90
Write the complete market potential formula showing all five inputs. Then explain the MDI formula and what an MDI of 40 tells a manager compared to an MDI of 75.
As you read, distinguish market potential in units from market potential in dollars, then connect MDI to how fully the market has been developed. Gate 2 will require both numeric calculation and interpretation.
7
MSI and Leakage Analysis
Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides
pp. 104–106
Using the chapter's five share-performance percentages for awareness, attractiveness, price acceptability, intentions, and purchase, how do you calculate MSI, how do you calculate leakage at each node, and which node creates the greatest leakage?
Work the chain multiplicatively, not additively. The largest leakage node is the managerial priority, and Gate 2 asks you to compute both the final MSI and specific leakages exactly.
8
SPI, SDI, and the Growth Opportunity Portfolio
Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides
pp. 106–109
Using the chapter's desired-performance levels, how do you calculate Share Potential Index, how do you calculate SDI from MSI and SPI, and how do MDI and SDI together determine the correct quadrant and strategy in the Growth Opportunity Portfolio?
Treat this as the last link in the analytical chain: MSI leads to SPI and SDI, and MDI plus SDI together determine the portfolio recommendation. Gate 2 tests both the math and the strategic prescription.
⚠️
High-Risk Reading Traps
Three Chapter 3 ideas consistently create confusion unless you write out the logic:
  • Narrow focus is not always myopia: the chapter uses Red Bull to show that a narrow market definition can be deliberate strategy rather than blindness, depending on intent and awareness.
  • Do not collapse the indexes into one measure: MDI, MSI, SPI, and SDI each diagnose a different problem, so mixing them up produces the wrong managerial conclusion.
  • NMC does not peak when demand peaks: the product life cycle discussion is explicit that marketing contribution and total market demand crest at different stages, and the IBM example shows how badly firms can underestimate true potential.
