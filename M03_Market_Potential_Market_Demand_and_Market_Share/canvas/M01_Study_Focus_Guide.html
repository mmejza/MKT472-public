This guide tells you exactly what to look for while reading Chapter 1. Questions 1–5 target chapter-exclusive content — material that appears only in the textbook and is not covered in the slides or lecture. Gate 1 tests these five constructs directly; students who skip the reading and rely on slides alone will not pass Gate 1.

Questions 6–8 target Gate 2, which requires synthesis of chapter reading with slide content. These are numeric entry questions — you need exact values from the chapter to calculate correct answers. Record the numbers you find.

This guide does not reveal Gate 1 or Gate 2 answers. It tells you which pages matter and what to record so you enter each gate prepared.

Reading Strategy for Chapter 1
🔢
Record every number
Gate 2 has numeric entry questions. When the chapter gives a retention rate, a profit multiple, or a customer count — write it down. You cannot retrieve it during the timed gate.
📋
Name the classification types
Chapter 1 introduces specific loyalty types with specific names. Gate 1 uses those exact labels. "Loyal customers who are hard to retain" is not the same as the chapter's term — know the vocabulary precisely.
⚖️
Look for comparisons
The chapter repeatedly contrasts pairs: forward-looking vs. backward-looking, retention vs. loyalty, transactional vs. relational. Gate 1 tests whether you can tell these apart, not just define them in isolation.
🏦
Study the MBNA case closely
One Gate 1 question draws entirely from the MBNA case study near pp. 18–19. The specific numbers in that case — retention rates, profit multiples, industry rankings — are tested. Read it twice and record exactly what changed and why.
Focus Questions
Gate 1 · Chapter Only — Questions 1–5
1
Gate 1 · Chapter Only
Market Orientation: Three Components
The chapter defines market orientation through three specific behavioral components. What are they — by exact name — and which one explicitly coordinates activities across departments rather than focusing outward on customers or competitors? The chapter also distinguishes transactional from relational marketing: what is the key strategic difference between the two?
📍 Chapter 1 · Opening sections · pp. 4–7
Record: the three component names exactly as the chapter states them — paraphrases will not match Gate 1 answer options. Note which component is internal-facing.
2
Gate 1 · Chapter Only
Zone of Tolerance
The chapter introduces the concept of a "zone of tolerance" in the context of customer satisfaction measurement. What are the two performance levels that define its boundaries? What happens when a customer's experience falls within the zone — do they complain or defect? What happens when performance falls below the lower boundary?
📍 Chapter 1 · Satisfaction measurement section · pp. 10–13
Record: the exact names of the two boundary levels and the customer behavior associated with each zone. Gate 1 may ask you to apply this concept to a scenario — you need to know the thresholds, not just the term.
3
Gate 1 · Chapter Only
Leading vs. Lagging Performance Metrics ⚠️ Counterintuitive
The chapter draws a sharp distinction between backward-looking (lagging) and forward-looking (leading) performance metrics. Which category does sales revenue belong to — and why does the chapter argue this makes it an unreliable primary indicator for managers who want to predict future performance? Which customer metrics does the chapter classify as forward-looking, and why?
📍 Chapter 1 · Customer metrics section · pp. 14–16
Why counterintuitive: Most managers treat revenue and market share as their primary performance signals. The chapter argues these are outcomes of prior customer experience decisions — by the time they change, the damage is done. Gate 1 may ask you to classify a given metric or explain why a lagging indicator cannot predict a future problem in time to correct it.
4
Gate 1 · Chapter Only
Loyalty Classification: Misfits and Spinners ⚠️ Counterintuitive
The chapter classifies customers into five loyalty types. Two of those types — misfits and spinners — appear profitable on the surface but are identified as unprofitable to retain. What defines each type? The chapter also distinguishes between captive customers and repeat customers — how does a captive customer differ from a truly loyal one, and why does this distinction matter strategically?
📍 Chapter 1 · Loyalty classification section · pp. 20–23 (Figure 1-14)
Why counterintuitive: High repeat-purchase rates suggest loyalty — but the chapter shows that some repeat buyers are captive (no alternatives) or spinners (high-frequency, low-value). Gate 1 may present a customer scenario and ask you to identify the loyalty type, or ask which type the firm should not invest in retaining.
5
Gate 1 · Chapter Only
MBNA: Retention Leverage and Profit Impact
The chapter presents the MBNA case study to illustrate the financial leverage of small retention improvements. By how many percentage points did MBNA improve its customer retention rate — and what was the resulting effect on profit? What was MBNA's industry ranking before and after the improvement? What does this case imply about the relationship between retention rate and long-run firm profitability?
📍 Chapter 1 · pp. 18–19
Record exactly: the retention rate before (%), the retention rate after (%), the profit multiplier (how many times), and the industry rank change. Gate 1 may ask what changed, what resulted, or what the case implies about retention leverage. All four numbers are testable.
Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides Synthesis — Questions 6–8
6
Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides
CSI Scale Values and Weighted Average
The chapter defines a Customer Satisfaction Index (CSI) and provides a specific numerical scale for each satisfaction level. What is the CSI value assigned to each of the five categories — Very Satisfied, Satisfied, Somewhat Satisfied, Somewhat Dissatisfied, and Dissatisfied? Gate 2 will give you a percentage distribution across these five groups and ask you to calculate a weighted-average CSI. Practice the formula: CSI = Σ (% in each group × CSI value for that group).
📍 Chapter 1 · CSI section · pp. 8–10 · Figure 1-3
Record: the five CSI scale values — VS, S, SS, SD, D — as exact numbers. Gate 2 Q1 is a numeric entry question using these values. If you don't have them memorized, you cannot compute the answer during the timed gate. Cross-check: do the slides confirm these values? They should match the chapter exactly.
7
Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides
Complaint-Resolution Model: Retention Rate Differential
The chapter presents a tree model for complaint resolution that tracks what happens to dissatisfied customers depending on whether they complain and whether their complaint is resolved. What retention rates does the chapter assign to: (a) dissatisfied customers who complain and are resolved, (b) dissatisfied customers who complain and are not resolved, and (c) dissatisfied customers who never complain? Gate 2 Q3 asks you to calculate the incremental customers retained when a firm motivates more dissatisfied customers to complain.
📍 Chapter 1 · Complaint resolution section · pp. 15–17 · Figure 1-7
Record: the three retention rates from the complaint tree. The key calculation is the retention differential — the gap between the complain-and-resolved rate and the never-complained rate. That differential, multiplied by the number of newly motivated complainers, gives the incremental customers retained.
8
Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides
Customer Life Expectancy: N = 1 ÷ (1 − r)
The chapter derives a formula for customer life expectancy (N) as a function of retention rate (r): N = 1 ÷ (1 − r). Where does this formula come from — what mathematical relationship between retention rate and the expected number of periods a customer remains does the chapter describe? Gate 2 Q4 gives you a specific retention rate and asks you to compute N. What would N be if r = 0.80? If r = 0.90?
📍 Chapter 1 · Customer life expectancy section · pp. 24–26 · Figure 1-16
Practice now: r = 0.80 → N = 1 ÷ (1 − 0.80) = 1 ÷ 0.20 = 5 years. r = 0.90 → N = 10 years. Gate 2 will give you a retention rate between 0.75 and 0.88 depending on your version. Work through the formula once with your own numbers so the mechanics are automatic during the timed gate.
⚡ Highest-Probability Gate 1 Targets
These three topics appear in almost every Gate 1 version. If you understand them cold, you will pass Gate 1 — no matter which variant you receive.
  • 1
    The MBNA Case (pp. 18–19) Every Gate 1 version includes at least one question about MBNA. The specific numbers — 90%→95% retention, 16-fold profit increase, 38th→4th ranking — are tested. Do not rely on your general memory of "retention matters." Know the exact case facts.
  • 2
    Misfits and Spinners — Loyalty Classification Gate 1 frequently presents a customer scenario and asks which loyalty type it represents, or asks which type a manager should not invest in retaining. Know all five types, their definitions, and which two are subtypes of unprofitable customers.
  • 3
    Forward-Looking vs. Backward-Looking Metrics This construct tests a genuine conceptual distinction — not a definition. Gate 1 may ask you to classify a specific metric (e.g., "current quarter revenue") or explain why a lagging indicator cannot prevent the customer problem it reflects. Understand the logic, not just the labels.
