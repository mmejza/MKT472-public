Market Potential, Market Demand and Market Share

How you define your market determines what you can see — and what you cannot see cannot be managed.

Part II · Market Analysis 195 points Two-Gate Accountability
Central Question
What this module asks you to figure out
How do you define a market well enough to diagnose market potential, demand, and share without fooling yourself?

This module builds three analytical tools — the Market Development Index (MDI), the Market Share Index (MSI), and the Share Development Index (SDI) — and shows how to use them together to diagnose a company's actual strategic position. A firm reporting 53% market share may be describing competitive dominance or a self-serving market definition. These tools distinguish between them.

The analytical challenge is that MDI, MSI, and SDI are each straightforward to calculate. The difficulty is using all three simultaneously to make a strategic diagnosis. A firm can have high MSI and low MDI, or vice versa — and each combination implies a different strategic response. Knowing which index to act on, and why, is the core skill this module develops.

The case company, Vantara Outdoors, presents a scenario where the "obvious" metric — market share — leads to the wrong conclusion until the full index analysis is done. That is the analytical trap this module is designed to help you avoid.

!
Analytical framing

The same sales figure can represent dominant share in one market definition and negligible share in another. Before you trust a market share number, ask how the market is defined.

Chapter-Exclusive Content

    Marketing Myopia (Levitt)

  • 2
    Red Bull's Deliberate Narrow-Market Strategy
    pp. 84-85 — Red Bull holds 41.7% of the worldwide energy drink market — but only 0.7% of the worldwide nonalcoholic beverage market. The same sales, two very different pictures. Named competitors and their brands are listed.

  • 5
    NMC Trajectory Over the Product Life Cycle
    pp. 97-98, 102 — Net Marketing Contribution is negative in introduction, peaks in late growth — before market demand peaks at maturity — then declines. IBM once estimated the total market for computers at 50 units per year. Both facts are chapter-exclusive.
Two Traps to Watch For

This module contains two specific calculation errors and strategic misunderstandings that students make frequently. Both are addressed explicitly in the lecture video and the apps. Know them before you start.

Trap 1 - Trusting a Market Share Figure Without Checking Market Definition

The same sales figure can represent dominant share in one market definition and negligible share in another.

Red Bull is the example: 41.7% of the energy drink market versus 0.7% of the broader nonalcoholic beverage market. The 53% share question in the framing above is the exact trap the case sets.

Where to find it: Chapter 3's broad-vs-narrow definition discussion, the Red Bull example, and the case framing.

Trap 2 - Treating MDI Alone as the Strategic Answer

MDI alone is insufficient — SDI is what reveals the opportunity. Students who calculate only one or two of the three indexes consistently make the wrong strategic recommendation in the case.

The three-index analysis is the core skill. The wrong shortcut is to stop at one number and assume the strategy is obvious.

Where to find it: The study tips, App 3, and the Vantara Outdoors case all force the full MDI/MSI/SDI analysis.
