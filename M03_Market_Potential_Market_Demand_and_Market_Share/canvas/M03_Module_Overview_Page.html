Module Overview

This module builds three analytical tools — the Market Development Index (MDI), the Market Share Index (MSI), and the Share Development Index (SDI) — and shows how to use them together to diagnose a company's actual strategic position. A firm reporting 53% market share may be describing competitive dominance or a self-serving market definition. These tools distinguish between them.

The analytical challenge is that MDI, MSI, and SDI are each straightforward to calculate. The difficulty is using all three simultaneously to make a strategic diagnosis. A firm can have high MSI and low MDI, or vice versa — and each combination implies a different strategic response. Knowing which index to act on, and why, is the core skill this module develops.

The case company, Vantara Outdoors, presents a scenario where the "obvious" metric — market share — leads to the wrong conclusion until the full index analysis is done. That is the analytical trap this module is designed to help you avoid.

⚠ Prerequisite Check — M02 NMC Familiarity Helpful

This module does not require NMC calculations, but familiarity with how market-level metrics connect to profitability (from Module 2) will help you interpret the MDI and SDI results in context. If you skipped M02, the formulas here are self-contained.

What the Chapter Covers Beyond the Slide Deck

Gate 1 tests these topics. They do not appear on the slides. You must find them in Chapter 3 before taking Gate 1.

  • 1
    Marketing Myopia (Levitt)
    pp. 81–82 — How a narrow, product-focused market definition limits growth and creates strategic blind spots — named and defined by Levitt, not just implied.
  • 2
    Red Bull's Deliberate Narrow-Market Strategy
    pp. 84–85 — Red Bull holds 41.7% of the worldwide energy drink market — but only 0.7% of the worldwide nonalcoholic beverage market. The same sales, two very different pictures. Named competitors and their brands are listed.
  • 3
    Three Innovation Types
    pp. 88–89 — Continuous, disruptive, and discontinuous innovation — each defined with specific historical examples. The slide deck does not name or define these types.
  • 4
    Seven Forces Restricting Market Development
    pp. 92–94 — The chapter names and defines seven specific forces — including Insufficient Infrastructure and Unavailability — that prevent markets from reaching their potential. The slide deck shows five with different labels.
  • 5
    NMC Trajectory Over the Product Life Cycle
    pp. 97–98, 102 — Net Marketing Contribution is negative in introduction, peaks in late growth — before market demand peaks at maturity — then declines. IBM once estimated the total market for computers at 50 units per year. Both facts are chapter-exclusive.

Before You Begin — Study Tips
  • Read Chapter 3 actively. The slide deck and lecture video deliberately leave out five topics that Gate 1 tests — including the Levitt marketing myopia definition, Red Bull's exact share figures, and the seven forces restricting market development. Find all five before you take Gate 1.
  • The three-index analysis is the core skill. MDI alone is insufficient — SDI is what reveals the opportunity. Students who calculate only one or two of the three indexes consistently make the wrong strategic recommendation in the case.
  • Complete App 3 before the case. The Market Share Performance Tree scenario in App 3 mirrors the Vantara Outdoors structure. Run it fully before opening the case.
  • Show your calculation steps. The case rubric awards partial credit only when intermediate steps are visible. Show the formula, your inputs, and the resulting index value — not just the final number.
  • Ask how the market is defined before you trust any market share figure. The 53% share question in the framing above is the exact trap the case sets. Before you accept any market share figure as meaningful, identify the market definition used to calculate it.
