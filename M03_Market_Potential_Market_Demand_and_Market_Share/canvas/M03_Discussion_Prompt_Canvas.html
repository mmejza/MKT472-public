Background

Post to Canvas Discussion before the deadline. Read the scenario carefully — both executives are working from real data. Your job is to decide which data matters more, and why.

Vantara Outdoors has been running an internal strategic debate for three months. Two members of the senior leadership team have reached opposite conclusions from the same data. Here is what each one said at the most recent planning meeting:

The Leadership Debate

VP of Sales
Broaden the definition

"Look at our numbers. Our Market Share Index is around 8% — and our actual dollar share is 8%. Those are essentially the same. The gap between what we should be capturing and what we're actually getting is negligible.

Meanwhile, our MDI is 66.7. That tells me we're already past the halfway point in our market's development. The crowding is coming. I'd rather invest now in new product development and move into adjacent outdoor recreation categories — cycling, climbing, paddling — where the market is larger and we can establish a position before others do.

The share-point value in the broad market is $280 million per percentage point. In our current narrow market it's only $24 million. That's an 11.7× difference. We should be playing for the bigger prize."

CMO
Fix share gaps first

"With respect, that's exactly backwards. Our MSI is 8.1% — not because the market is crowded, but because 62% of our potential customers have never heard of us. Sixty-two percent. Our awareness leakage alone is larger than our entire market share.

Our SDI is 35.9, which means we're achieving barely a third of our share potential in the market we already understand and already serve. Broadening our market definition doesn't fix that problem — it hides it. We would go from 8% dollar share in a market we know to 0.69% share in a market we don't.

And the broad market's MDI is 73.7 — even more developed than ours. I want to fix the awareness gap first. Then we can talk about expansion."

Both executives are citing real numbers from Vantara's data. Neither is wrong about the numbers. They disagree about which numbers should drive the decision.

What a Strong Post Looks Like

Strong Posts Will
  1. State a clear side in the first sentence — not "both sides have valid points." Pick one and defend it.
  2. Cite at least two specific numbers correctly — e.g., MDI 66.7, SDI 35.9, awareness leakage 62%, share-point $24M vs. $280M — and explain what each number implies, not just report it.
  3. Engage the opposing argument directly — acknowledge what the other executive gets right, then explain why it is outweighed by the data supporting your position.
  4. Write a genuine devil's advocate section — make the strongest possible case against your own position using specific numbers from this module, not a perfunctory "the other side says…" summary.
Posts That Will Not Receive Full Credit
  • "Both sides have valid points" — this is not a position.
  • "The broad market is bigger so the VP is right" — correct observation, but misses the MDI and SDI analysis.
  • "We should fix awareness first because marketing is important" — 62% leakage and SDI 35.9 must be cited, not just implied.
  • "The CMO is right because share development is always more efficient" — generic principle without Vantara-specific data.
  • A devil's advocate section that only summarizes "the other side says X" without making the strongest possible case against your own argument using module data.

Grading Note — The "Correct" Side

The module's answer key supports the CMO's position: SDI 35.9 and the higher broad MDI (73.7) both argue for share development before market expansion. However, a student who sides with the VP and makes a rigorous quantitative argument — citing the 11.7× share-point differential, acknowledging the MDI comparison, and arguing that first-mover positioning in adjacent markets before saturation outweighs the SDI gap — can receive full credit. What cannot receive full credit is a position taken without engaging both metrics. The rubric penalizes lack of quantitative grounding, not the choice of side.
