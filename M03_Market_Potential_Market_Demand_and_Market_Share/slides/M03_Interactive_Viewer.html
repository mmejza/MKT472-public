LO1

Levels of Market Definition

A market consists of buyers who have the desire and ability to purchase a product that satisfies a need. Companies must clearly define their market to identify competitors and position products effectively. Markets exist at three levels:

Generic Market

Broadest level. A broad, heterogeneous group of buyers of substitutable product categories satisfying a similar general need.

Example: Non-alcoholic beverages (milk, soft drinks, water, juice, energy drinks)

Product Category Market

Middle level. Buyers choosing from a range of product forms within a specific category.

Example: Milk buyers choosing from whole, 2%, 1%, and skim milk options

Product Form Market

Narrowest level. Buyers selecting a specific product form within one category.

Example: Buyers of flavored sparkling water (e.g., LaCroix)

Why This Framework Matters

Competitor Identification
Narrowing to product form reveals true competitors. LaCroix competes with sparkling water brands, not all soft drinks — even though both are in the same generic beverage market.
Growth Opportunity Spotting
Seeing the broader generic market reveals adjacent categories for expansion. LaCroix could consider entering the non-cola soft drink market.

Broad vs. Narrow Market Definition

Broad Definition Advantage (Coca-Cola model)

  • See all substitute products and adjacent markets
  • Identify untapped growth opportunities
  • Avoid "marketing myopia" (narrow product focus)
  • Compete in fast-growing categories (energy +31%, sports +23%)
Narrow Definition Strategy (Red Bull model)

  • Concentrate resources on one category
  • Build dominant share in a specific segment (Red Bull: 41.7% of world energy drinks)
  • Defensible leadership position
  • Risk: missing adjacent opportunities

Market Potential

Market Potential = the maximum level of demand possible in a market. It equals the sum of current buyers' demand plus future demand from new buyers not yet in the market.

Market Potential (units) = Max. Consuming Units × Buying Ceiling × Purchase Rate × Qty per Purchase
Market Potential ($) = Market Potential (units) × Average Price

How Innovation Changes Market Potential

Continuous


Ongoing improvements to technology and products within the same category. Examples: faster PCs, thinner TVs.

Effect: Gradual expansion of market potential.
Disruptive


Innovations that make products smaller, cheaper, or easier to use, attracting many new customers. Examples: microcomputer, ballpoint pen.

Effect: Sharp jump in market potential.
Discontinuous


Major technology shifts that create entirely new markets. Examples: digital photography replacing film, flat-panel TVs replacing CRT.

Effect: Resets market potential entirely.

Market Development Index (MDI) & Market Growth

MDI = (Current Market Demand ÷ Market Potential) × 100
MDI < 33
High growth potential — early-stage market. Address awareness and product gaps.
MDI 33–67
Moderate development — mainstream market growth. Focus on price, distribution, and improvements.
MDI > 67
Near maturity — growth becomes increasingly difficult. Competitive pressure intensifies.

LO3

Product Life Cycle (PLC)

The PLC describes the pattern of total sales and profit in a market over time, typically progressing through four stages:

Introduction
Demand: Grows slowly; few buyers
Profit: Negative — marketing costs exceed revenues
Buyers: Innovators and early adopters only
Key Goal: Build awareness; educate market
Pricing: Skim (high price) or Penetration (low price)
Example: iPhone in 2007; EV trucks in 2022
Growth
Demand: Accelerates; early majority enters
Profit: Turns positive; peaks in late growth
Buyers: Early majority; triers still dominant
Key Goal: Differentiate; build market share
Pricing: Competitive; deal pricing
Example: Smartphones 2009–2015
Maturity
Demand: Peaks; growth slows to near zero
Profit: Declining; intense price competition
Buyers: Mostly repeat buyers; sophisticated
Key Goal: Defend market share; retain customers
Pricing: Defend share; maximize profitability
Example: Personal computers 2015–present
Decline
Demand: Continuously shrinks
Profit: Continues to fall; milk what's left
Buyers: Laggards; loyal holdouts
Key Goal: Harvest profits or delete product
Pricing: Stay profitable
Example: DVD players; film cameras

Price, Cost & Margin Trends Over the PLC

  • Both unit price and unit cost decline over the PLC as competition intensifies and experience accumulates.
  • Price falls faster than cost, so gross margin (price – cost) shrinks over time.
  • Marketing & sales expenses peak near maturity and decrease in decline.
  • Net marketing contribution (NMC) is negative in introduction, peaks in late growth, and declines through maturity and decline.

Market Share & Market Share Index (MSI)

Unit Market Share = (Company Unit Sales ÷ Total Market Unit Sales) × 100
Dollar Market Share = (Company $ Sales ÷ Total Market $ Sales) × 100
Key Rule: Unit market share = dollar market share only when the company's price equals the average market price. Always calculate both.

Share Development Index (SDI) & Growth Opportunity Portfolio

Share Potential Index = Desired Awareness × Desired Attractive × Desired Price OK × Desired Intent × Desired Purchase
SDI = (MSI ÷ Share Potential Index) × 100
