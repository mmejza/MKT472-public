Why this guide exists

Chapter 1 contains content that is tested in two different places in this module — and the distinction matters. Gate 1 tests chapter-only material: concepts, frameworks, and cases that appear in the textbook but are intentionally absent from the slide deck and lecture. Students who skip the reading and rely on slides alone will not be able to answer Gate 1 questions correctly.

Gate 2 tests synthesis: it combines chapter data with slide deck content and requires calculation. Some Gate 2 questions use numerical data that only appears in the chapter. This guide tells you exactly which questions to answer while reading, what to record, and where to find the content.

Work through these eight questions as you read — not after. Write your answers down. The goal is active reading, not passive scanning.

Reading strategy for Chapter 1
✏️
Work the numbers as you read
Chapter 1 introduces the CSI weighted average, the complaint-resolution model, the N formula, and CLV. Do not just read past the tables — reproduce each calculation on paper before moving on. Gate 2 will ask you to compute these from different inputs.
🗂
Record exact names and definitions
Gate 1 tests precise vocabulary: the three behavioral components of market orientation, the five loyalty types, and the zone of tolerance. The exact names matter. Write them down as you encounter them — do not paraphrase.
⚠️
Flag counterintuitive results before reveals
Two sections in Chapter 1 produce results that contradict common intuition. Before you read the chapter's answer, write down your own prediction. When the chapter contradicts it, that is the concept Gate 2 and the case analysis will test.
📌
Mark chapter-exclusive figures
Several figures in Chapter 1 do not appear in the slide deck: the zone of tolerance diagram, the loyalty classification matrix, Fig. 1-8 (retention profit leverage), and the MBNA case data. These are Gate 1 targets. Mark them as you read.
Eight focus questions — answer while reading
1
Gate 1  ·  Chapter Only
What are the three behavioral components of market orientation, and how does the chapter distinguish between transactional and relational marketing? Which component most directly requires collaboration across departments?
Ch. 1  ·  Opening sections Record all three component names exactly. Note the specific departments or activities the chapter associates with each. The transactional vs. relational distinction appears in the same section — write one sentence for each.
2
Gate 1  ·  Chapter Only
What is the zone of tolerance, and where does it sit relative to a customer's minimum acceptable service level and their desired service level? Can a customer be inside the zone of tolerance and still have a CSI score below 100?
Ch. 1  ·  Satisfaction measurement section Draw a quick diagram showing the three levels: desired, zone of tolerance, minimum acceptable. The zone of tolerance does not appear in the slide deck — Gate 1 will ask you to identify or apply it from the chapter alone.
3
Gate 1  ·  Chapter Only
The chapter classifies performance metrics as either forward-looking or backward-looking. Which side of that distinction do customer satisfaction, retention rate, and loyalty fall on — and which side do sales revenue and market share fall on? Why does the distinction matter strategically?
Ch. 1  ·  Customer metrics section Make a two-column list: forward-looking metrics on the left, backward-looking on the right. The chapter's argument about why forward-looking metrics are more strategically valuable is the key insight here — write it in one sentence.
4
Gate 1  ·  Chapter Only
Chapter 1 identifies five types of customers in a loyalty classification system. What are the two subtypes of unprofitable customers, and what distinguishes a "misfit" from a "spinner"? Under what condition does the chapter suggest a firm should consider abandoning a customer segment?
Ch. 1  ·  Loyalty classification section List all five loyalty types. Pay special attention to misfits and spinners — both are unprofitable but for different reasons. The chapter's strategic recommendation for handling each is a likely Gate 1 scenario question.
5
Gate 1  ·  Chapter Only
The MBNA case shows that improving customer retention from 90% to 95% produced a 16-fold increase in profits and moved the firm from 38th to 4th in industry ranking. Why does a 5-percentage-point retention improvement produce such a disproportionately large profit increase — what is the mechanism?
Ch. 1  ·  pp. 18–19 Record the three specific outcomes the chapter cites: the profit multiplier (16×), the ranking change (38th → 4th), and the retention change (90% → 95%). Then write the chapter's explanation of why small retention gains produce large profit gains. ⚠️ Counterintuitive — a 5-point gain driving 16× profit is not linear
6
Gate 2  ·  Chapter + Slides
Using the six satisfaction tiers and their CSI values (Very Satisfied = 100, Satisfied = 85, Somewhat Satisfied = 70, Somewhat Dissatisfied = 50, Dissatisfied = 30, Very Dissatisfied = 0), calculate the weighted-average CSI for a company whose customer distribution is: 20% VS, 35% S, 25% SS, 12% SD, 8% D, 0% VD.
Ch. 1  ·  CSI section  ·  Fig. 1-4 Practice the weighted-average formula now: multiply each CSI value by its percentage weight (as a decimal) and sum across all tiers. Gate 2 will give you a different distribution — the method is what you are learning, not this specific answer.
7
Gate 2  ·  Chapter + Slides
Chapter 1 demonstrates that eliminating all "somewhat dissatisfied" customers from a company's base does not improve — and in fact reduces — average customer profit. What happens to the weighted-average CSI and to average profit when this segment is removed, and why does the result defy most managers' initial intuition?
Ch. 1  ·  Distributions section  ·  Fig. 1-5 Record the before and after: CSI score with the segment included vs. excluded, and average profit with vs. without. The chapter explains the mechanism — write it down. This is the module's central cognitive trap and Gate 2 will test the analytical version of it. ⚠️ Counterintuitive — removing "bad" customers makes the average worse
8
Gate 2  ·  Chapter + Slides
Using the formula N = 1 ÷ (1 − r), what is the customer life expectancy for a company with an 85% monthly retention rate? Then reverse the problem: if a company's average customer lifetime is 8.33 periods, what is its retention rate?
Ch. 1  ·  Retention section  ·  CLV formula derivation Solve both directions: N from r, and r from N. Gate 2 will give you one or the other — you need fluency in both. The inverse formula is r = 1 − (1 ÷ N). Record the formulas and your worked solutions.
🎯 Highest-probability Gate 1 targets

Three constructs from Chapter 1 appear most frequently across Gate 1 question variants. These are worth extra attention while reading:

The zone of tolerance — its definition, its boundaries relative to the CSI scale, and what happens to customer behavior when service falls below the lower boundary. This is chapter-exclusive and will not be in the slides.

Misfits and spinners — the two subtypes of unprofitable customers in the loyalty classification system, and the strategic distinction between them. Know the names, the definitions, and the recommended response to each.

The MBNA retention leverage result — the specific numbers (90% → 95%, 16-fold profit increase, 38th → 4th ranking) and the chapter's explanation of why a small retention gain produces a disproportionately large profit impact. Gate 1 will ask about the mechanism, not just the statistics.
