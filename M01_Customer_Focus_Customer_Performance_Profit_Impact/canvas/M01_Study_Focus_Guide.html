Reading Strategy for Chapter 1
✏️
Work the formulas as you read
Chapter 1 introduces weighted-average CSI, the complaint-resolution logic, the N formula, and CLV setup. Reproduce each calculation on paper instead of reading past the tables. Gate 2 changes the numbers, not the method.
🗂️
Record exact chapter-only terms
Gate 1 depends on precise labels: the three behavioral components of market orientation, the five loyalty types, and the zone of tolerance. Write those names down exactly as the chapter states them.
⚠️
Stop at the counterintuitive results
Two chapter sections are designed to violate instinct: the retention-profit leverage result and the distribution example where removing weaker customers makes the average worse. Predict the result before reading the explanation.
📌
Mark figures that do not appear on slides
The zone of tolerance diagram, the loyalty classification matrix, the retention-profit leverage figure, and MBNA case details are Gate 1 targets because they stay in the chapter. Flag them as chapter-exclusive while you read.
Eight Focus Questions — Answer These While You Read
1
Market Orientation and Relational Marketing
Gate 1 · Chapter Only
Ch. 1 · Opening sections
What are the three behavioral components of market orientation, and how does the chapter distinguish between transactional and relational marketing? Which component most directly requires collaboration across departments?
Record all three component names exactly. Note the specific departments or activities the chapter associates with each. Write one sentence distinguishing transactional from relational marketing in the same section.
2
Zone of Tolerance
Gate 1 · Chapter Only
Ch. 1 · Satisfaction measurement section
What is the zone of tolerance, and where does it sit relative to a customer's minimum acceptable service level and desired service level? Can a customer be inside the zone of tolerance and still have a CSI score below 100?
Draw a quick three-level diagram: desired service, zone of tolerance, and minimum acceptable service. This concept stays in the chapter and does not appear in the slide deck.
3
Forward-Looking vs. Backward-Looking Metrics
Gate 1 · Chapter Only
Ch. 1 · Customer metrics section
The chapter classifies performance metrics as either forward-looking or backward-looking. Which side of that distinction do customer satisfaction, retention rate, and loyalty fall on, and which side do sales revenue and market share fall on? Why does the distinction matter strategically?
Make a two-column list as you read. The key idea is not just which metrics go where, but why the chapter treats one category as more strategically valuable.
4
Misfits, Spinners, and Loyalty Types
Gate 1 · Chapter Only
Ch. 1 · Loyalty classification section
Chapter 1 identifies five types of customers in a loyalty classification system. What are the two subtypes of unprofitable customers, and what distinguishes a misfit from a spinner? Under what condition does the chapter suggest a firm should consider abandoning a customer segment?
List all five loyalty types for context, then focus on the strategic distinction between misfits and spinners and the chapter's recommended response to each.
5
MBNA Retention Leverage Result
Gate 1 · Chapter Only ⚠️ Counterintuitive
Ch. 1 · pp. 18–19
The MBNA case shows that improving customer retention from 90% to 95% produced a 16-fold increase in profits and moved the firm from 38th to 4th in industry ranking. Why does a 5-percentage-point retention improvement produce such a disproportionately large profit increase, and what mechanism does the chapter use to explain it?
Record the three cited outcomes exactly: 90% to 95%, 16-fold profit increase, and 38th to 4th ranking. Gate 1 tests the mechanism behind the leverage, not just the numbers.
Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides Synthesis
6
Weighted-Average CSI Calculation
Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides
Ch. 1 · CSI section · Fig. 1-4
Using the six satisfaction tiers and their CSI values, calculate the weighted-average CSI for a company whose customer distribution is 20% Very Satisfied, 35% Satisfied, 25% Somewhat Satisfied, 12% Somewhat Dissatisfied, 8% Dissatisfied, and 0% Very Dissatisfied.
Practice the weighted-average method while you read: multiply each CSI value by its percentage weight as a decimal and sum across the tiers. Gate 2 changes the distribution but relies on the same method.
7
Distribution Trap: Removing Dissatisfied Customers
Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides ⚠️ Counterintuitive
Ch. 1 · Distributions section · Fig. 1-5
Chapter 1 demonstrates that eliminating all somewhat dissatisfied customers from a company's base does not improve and can reduce average customer profit. What happens to the weighted-average CSI and to average profit when this segment is removed, and why does the result defy most managers' initial intuition?
Record the before-and-after CSI and average-profit values, then write down the chapter's explanation of the mechanism. This is the module's central cognitive trap.
8
N Formula and Retention Rate Conversion
Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides
Ch. 1 · Retention section · CLV derivation
Using the formula N = 1 ÷ (1 − r), what is the customer life expectancy for a company with an 85% monthly retention rate? Then reverse the problem: if a company's average customer lifetime is 8.33 periods, what is its retention rate?
Solve the formula in both directions as you read. Gate 2 may give you retention and ask for lifetime, or give you lifetime and ask for retention. Record both formulas and your worked setup.
⚠️
High-Risk Reading Traps
Three Chapter 1 ideas are easy to misread unless you stop and write them out:
  • Zone of tolerance: know where it sits between desired service and minimum acceptable service, and do not confuse it with a perfect CSI score.
  • Misfits versus spinners: both are unprofitable, but for different reasons, so the strategic response is not the same.
  • Retention and distribution effects: a small retention gain can create a huge profit change, while removing low-satisfaction segments can actually lower average profit because the remaining distribution shifts toward the middle, not the top.
