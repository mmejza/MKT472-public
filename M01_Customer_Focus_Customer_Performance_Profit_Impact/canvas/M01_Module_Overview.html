Market Orientation & Customer Lifetime Value

Firm profitability is not a function of product quality or price alone — it is a function of how well the firm understands, satisfies, and retains its customers over time.

Part I · Customer Focus 195 points Two-Gate Accountability
Central Question
What this module asks you to figure out
If eliminating your most dissatisfied customers improves your average satisfaction score, does it also improve your firm's profitability?

This module builds the quantitative and strategic foundations of market-oriented thinking. You will calculate Customer Satisfaction Indices, model the economics of complaint resolution, and compute Customer Lifetime Value using two different methods — then apply all three tools to an original case scenario.

The analytical challenge is called the distributions-versus-averages problem. When you eliminate a customer segment from a distribution, the average changes — but not always in the direction you expect. The module leads you to a specific prediction, then confronts you with data that contradicts it. That moment of cognitive disruption is the module's core learning event.

The case company, Meridian Mobile, presents a scenario where the "obvious" recommendation — implementing a complaint-resolution program — turns out to be financially unjustified when the math is done correctly. That counterintuitive result is the analytical trap this module is designed to help you see clearly.

!
Analytical framing

Averages conceal strategic information that distributions reveal. Before you trust a summary metric, ask what it is hiding about the underlying segment data.

Two Traps to Watch For

This module contains two specific analytical errors that students make frequently. Both are addressed directly in the apps and lecture video. Know them before you start the case.

Trap 1 — Assuming Removing Unprofitable Customers Improves Average Profit

When you eliminate a segment from a distribution, the weighted average recalculates across the remaining segments. Removing low-satisfaction, low-profit customers raises the average satisfaction score — but can reduce or hold flat the average profit figure, because those customers were still contributing some gross margin above zero.

The App 1 "No Dissatisfied Customers" preset is designed to trigger this exact surprise. Run that preset and record what happens to average profit before you open the case.

Where to find it: App 1, Slides 6–7 (setup + reveal), and Case Part 1.

Trap 2 — Recommending the Complaint-Resolution Program Without Calculating the ROI

The complaint-resolution program sounds compelling: dissatisfied customers who complain are retained at a 75% rate vs. 14% without contact. But the incremental annual profit from the program (~$117,000) is less than the program's annual cost ($180,000). The program is not financially justified at the stated parameters.

Students who pattern-match to "complaint programs are good" rather than completing the ROI calculation will reach the wrong conclusion and lose points on Case Part 2 regardless of whether the Part 2 calculation itself is correct.

Where to find it: App 2 (break-even program cost calculator), Case Part 2, and Case Part 4 rubric.
