Background

Meridian Mobile is a regional mobile virtual network operator with 650,000 subscribers. The company has just completed a customer satisfaction survey and financial analysis using the tools from this module. The results are on the table when the CFO and Chief Marketing Officer sit down to discuss the quarterly strategy review.

The CFO's Position

Our dissatisfied customers will never be as profitable as our satisfied ones. The data makes that clear. The cost of running a complaint-resolution program is real and immediate — $180,000 per year. The benefit is speculative. We would be spending certain money chasing uncertain returns. I recommend we focus our budget on acquiring new, higher-value customers rather than trying to rescue unhappy ones. Every dollar we spend on dissatisfied customers is a dollar not spent growing our profitable base.

The CMO's Position

You're ignoring the retention math entirely. Every customer we lose costs us five times more to replace than to retain. Our dissatisfied customers who don't complain exit at an 86% rate — that's a massive drain on our base that doesn't show up anywhere in your acquisition model. Investing in complaint resolution is not speculative — it is the highest-ROI spend we can make. We retain more customers, reduce acquisition pressure, and improve our CLV simultaneously. The numbers support this.

Part 1 — Your Position

Take a clear position — side with either the CFO or the CMO. Using specific data and concepts from this module, defend your position in 150–200 words.

Part 2 — Devil's Advocate

Now make the strongest case for the position you just argued against. Use specific data from this module to support the opposing argument. What is the best evidence the other side has? Where is your own argument most vulnerable? (100–150 words)

What "Specific Data" Means

The best posts cite at least two specific numbers from the module — from the CSI table, the complaint-resolution calculator, the CLV formula, or the retention comparison tables in Chapter 1. Saying "retention is important" is not a data-supported argument. Saying "our complaint-resolution model shows that motivating 18% of dissatisfied customers to complain retains approximately 3,400 additional customers worth $850,000 annually — less than the $180,000 program cost" is a data-supported argument. The numbers matter.

A Note on the Right Answer

There is a defensible data-based answer to this question — one position the numbers more clearly support. You will encounter it in the Case Analysis. The discussion is intentionally structured to run before the case so that you form a position first, then test it against the full data set. Your discussion grade does not depend on taking the "correct" position — it depends on the quality of your reasoning and use of data.
