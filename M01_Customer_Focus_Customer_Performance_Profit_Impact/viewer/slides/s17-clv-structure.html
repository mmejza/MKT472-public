Two Things to Notice About CLV
Structural insights before the worked examples
CLV = [M × (r ÷ (1 + i − r))] − AC
Notice 1 — Two Terms, Two Levers
The first term [M × (r ÷ (1 + i − r))] is the present value of all future customer profits — a revenue-side lever. The second term AC is acquisition cost — a cost-side lever. CLV is positive only when lifetime profit exceeds acquisition cost.
To increase CLV: raise M, raise r, lower i, or lower AC — four independent levers.
Notice 2 — r in Numerator AND Denominator
The retention rate r appears in both the numerator (directly) and the denominator (as part of 1 + i − r). As r increases: the numerator rises (more profit) AND the denominator falls (smaller discount). CLV therefore grows at an accelerating rate — not a linear rate — as retention improves.
80%→85% produces a larger CLV gain than 60%→65%. Retention leverage is greatest at high retention rates.
Why This Matters for the MBNA Case

The MBNA result (a small retention improvement producing a disproportionately large profit increase) is a direct consequence of Notice 2. At high retention rates, further retention improvement generates accelerating CLV gains — which is exactly why the firm's profit response was non-linear. The mechanism is in Chapter 1, pp. 18–19 (Gate 1).
