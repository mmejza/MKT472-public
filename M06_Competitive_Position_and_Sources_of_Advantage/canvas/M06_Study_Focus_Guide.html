Reading Strategy for Chapter 6
📍
Mark the chapter-only figures
Flag Appendix 6.1, Figure 6-17, and Figure 6-24 before you start. Gate 1 pulls from each of them, and none of those answers are restated in the slides.
✍️
Write the formulas with direction rules
Do not just copy VCAI, RMS, and the industry-attractiveness logic. Next to each measure, write what values above or below the benchmark mean, because Gate 2 depends on interpretation, not calculation alone.
⚠️
Slow down at the counterintuitive parts
Two places trip students repeatedly: negative customer value can come from price, not product failure, and high exit barriers make an industry less attractive. Mark both passages and restate them in your own words.
🔗
Connect the frameworks before the slides
Chapter 6 links competitor signals, advantage measures, and industry attractiveness into one decision sequence. If you map how those tools fit together now, Gate 2 best-answer questions become much easier.
Eight Focus Questions — Answer These While You Read
1
Customer Value Map and the Fair Price Line
Gate 1 · Chapter Only Counterintuitive
pp. 191–192 + Appendix 6.1
According to the customer value map, what does it mean when a brand is priced above the fair price line, and what does that predict about customer response and future profitability?
Record the customer value formula and identify why a firm can create negative customer value even when the product itself is not weak.
2
Prisoner's Dilemma and Nash Equilibrium
Gate 1 · Chapter Only Counterintuitive
pp. 218–219 · Fig. 6-24
Which outcome in the payoff matrix is the Nash equilibrium, and why does each firm rationally choose that outcome even though both would earn more if both firms held price?
Sketch the four cells of the matrix and write one sentence explaining why "both hold price" is better jointly but unstable strategically.
3
Competitive Benchmarking Outside the Industry
Gate 1 · Chapter Only
pp. 215–216
What are the three steps in the competitive benchmarking process, and why does the chapter recommend benchmarking against a world-class performer outside your own industry rather than your nearest direct rival?
Write the steps in order, then note the Xerox and General Mills examples and what each firm learned by looking beyond its competitor set.
4
Competitor Intelligence Signals
Gate 1 · Chapter Only
p. 211 · Fig. 6-17
How does the chapter distinguish signals of financial distress from signals of marketing leadership failure, and why does it matter which type of weakness a competitor is showing?
List at least two behaviors from each category and connect each category to the kind of strategic opening it creates.
5
Sustainable Advantage Erosion
Gate 1 · Chapter Only
pp. 194–195
What common pattern explains why firms like GM, NBC, and Sears lost dominant positions, and what does Andrew Grove's "Only the Paranoid Survive" argument imply about how firms must manage competitive advantage over time?
Write a short note on what those firms failed to adapt to. The chapter's point is not that they stopped trying, but that competitive forces kept moving.
6
Variable Cost Advantage Index
Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides
Ch. 6 VCAI section + slides
Write out the Variable Cost Advantage Index formula, then explain what an index above 100 versus below 100 means about a firm's cost position relative to competitors.
Do not stop at the formula. Add the direction rule beside it so you can interpret a numeric result quickly when Gate 2 gives you a worked problem.
7
Industry Attractiveness and Exit Barriers
Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides Counterintuitive
Industry Attractiveness section + Slides S24–S27
For each Porter force used in the industry attractiveness model, does a higher score make the industry more attractive or less attractive, and why are exit barriers one of the most commonly misread forces?
Write all six force directions in a list. Then add one sentence explaining why high exit barriers keep firms trapped in low-profit competition.
8
Combining Industry Attractiveness with the Price-War Logic
Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides
pp. 218–219 + Industry Attractiveness section
If the Prisoner's Dilemma shows price-cutting pressure but the industry still scores above average on industry attractiveness, how should a manager combine those two signals when deciding whether to hold, enter, defend, or exit?
Draft a short synthesis answer. Gate 2 may not ask for a formula here; it may ask you which framework changes the recommendation and which one does not.
🎯 Highest-Probability Gate 1 Targets
If you are short on time, prioritize these three areas — they appear on Gate 1 across all question variants and cannot be answered from the slides alone:
  • Customer value map interpretation (Q1): Know what a brand above the fair price line implies and why negative customer value can appear even without a bad product.
  • Prisoner's Dilemma equilibrium logic (Q2): Know why mutual price-cutting is the Nash equilibrium even though both firms would earn more if both held price.
  • Competitor intelligence signal classification (Q4): Be able to separate financial distress from marketing leadership failure using the chapter's examples and signals.
