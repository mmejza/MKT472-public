Reading Strategy for Chapter 6
📍
Mark the Five Figures

Before reading, tab or highlight Fig 6-2 (competitive position–profitability), Fig 6-17 (competitor signals), Fig 6-24 (Prisoner's Dilemma payoff matrix), and Appendix 6.1 (customer value map). Gate 1 pulls from all four.

✍️
Write Down the Formulas

The VCAI, Differentiation Index, and RMS formulas appear in both the chapter and the slides — but the chapter shows the derivation logic. Write the formula and its direction rule (what does a value above 100 mean?) before viewing the slides.

⚠️
Slow Down at Exit Barriers

The direction of the exit barriers force in the IAI is the most commonly confused concept in this chapter. The chapter explains why. Read the explanation twice, write it in your own words, and check your understanding with Question 7 below.

🔗
Connect the Three Tools

Chapter 6 presents three analytical tools: competitive advantage indices (VCAI, DI, RMS), competitor intelligence signals, and industry attractiveness scoring. These are not independent — the case asks you to use all three together. Notice how the chapter connects them.

Eight Focus Questions — Answer These While Reading
Gate 1  ·  Chapter Only  ·  Questions 1–5
1
Gate 1 · Chapter Only
According to the customer value map in Appendix 6.1, what does it mean when a brand is priced above the fair price line — and what does that predict about the firm's customer retention and profitability?
📖 pp. 191–192 + Appendix 6.1 ⚠️ Counterintuitive
Record the formula: Customer Value = _______ − _______. Then identify which SUV brand in the appendix has the most negative customer value. The result may surprise you — negative customer value does not require a bad product.
2
Gate 1 · Chapter Only
In the Prisoner's Dilemma payoff matrix (Fig 6-24), which cell represents the Nash equilibrium — the outcome where neither firm can improve by changing its strategy — and why does each firm rationally choose that outcome even though both end up worse than if they had both held price?
📖 pp. 218–219, Fig 6-24 ⚠️ Counterintuitive
Draw the 2×2 matrix with the four contribution outcomes. Label each cell. Then identify which cell is the Nash equilibrium and write one sentence explaining why "both hold price" is NOT the equilibrium even though it produces better outcomes for both firms.
3
Gate 1 · Chapter Only
What is the three-step competitive benchmarking process described in the chapter, and why does it specify finding a world-class performer outside your industry rather than benchmarking against your nearest competitor?
📖 pp. 215–216
Write the three steps in order. For the Xerox example: what was the competitive weakness, which outside company did they benchmark against, and what category did that company belong to? Answer those three questions before moving on.
4
Gate 1 · Chapter Only
Figure 6-17 lists two distinct categories of competitor behavior signals. What is the difference between a signal of financial distress and a signal of marketing leadership failure — and why does it matter which category a competitor's behavior falls into?
📖 p. 211, Fig 6-17
List two behaviors from each category. Then answer: if a competitor is laying off employees and cutting trade show attendance, which category does that fall into — and what strategic window does it open for your firm?
5
Gate 1 · Chapter Only
The chapter describes how GM, NBC, and Sears each lost dominant competitive positions. What was the common cause of their erosion, and what is the core strategic implication of Andrew Grove's "Only the Paranoid Survive" thesis for how firms should manage competitive advantage?
📖 pp. 194–195
Write one sentence explaining why each firm lost its position — the cause was NOT poor product quality or price increases. Then write Grove's core argument in your own words. This is the chapter's answer to the question: how long does a competitive advantage last?
Gate 2  ·  Chapter + Slides  ·  Questions 6–8
6
Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides
Write out the Variable Cost Advantage Index formula from the chapter. Then apply it: if a firm's COGS is 52% of sales and the three nearest competitors have COGS of 58%, 61%, and 55% respectively, what is the VCAI and what does the resulting number mean?
📖 Ch. 6, VCAI section (see also Slide S6 and S10)
Gate 2 will give you a different set of numbers — but the same formula. Record: (1) the formula, (2) the direction rule (index below 100 means _______, index above 100 means _______), and (3) this worked answer so you can check your Gate 2 calculation for consistency.
7
Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides
For each of the six Porter forces, does a high level of that force make an industry more attractive or less attractive to compete in? List all six, and for any force where your initial intuition might be wrong, write a one-sentence explanation of the correct direction.
📖 Ch. 6, Industry Attractiveness section (see also Slides S24–S27)
Pay special attention to exit barriers — this is the most commonly confused force. The chapter explains why high exit barriers reduce attractiveness. Write the explanation in your own words before you see the slides. Gate 2 Q4 will ask you to calculate an IAI using a set of six force scores.
8
Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides
If a firm competes in an industry with an above-average Industry Attractiveness Index, and the payoff matrix shows that cutting price is the dominant strategy for both competitors, should the firm cut price? What does the IAI add to the Prisoner's Dilemma analysis?
📖 pp. 218–219, Fig 6-24 + Ch. 6 IAI section
Gate 2 Q8 is a best-answer question that integrates these two frameworks. The chapter doesn't give you a single formula for this — it requires reasoning. Draft your answer in 2–3 sentences now. Does a high IAI change the dominant strategy? Does it change the recommendation?
Highest-Probability Gate 1 Targets

Three topics appear in virtually every Gate 1 version. If you can answer these from memory before you start the quiz, you are well-positioned: (1) The customer value formula and what a negative customer value predicts (pp. 191–192); (2) why mutual price-cutting is the Nash equilibrium even though "both hold" is better for both firms (pp. 218–219); and (3) the correct category for a competitor that is cutting headcount and reducing trade show presence (Fig 6-17). None of these answers appear anywhere in the slides — they require reading.
