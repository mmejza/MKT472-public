Module Overview

This module develops the capacity to analyze competitive position analytically — not as a list of strategic options, but as measurable, quantifiable advantages with predictable profit implications. Every claim in a competitive strategy must be grounded in a number: a Variable Cost Advantage Index, a Differentiation Advantage score, a Relative Market Share ratio, an Industry Attractiveness Index.

The central intellectual challenge is the advantage selection problem: three legitimate sources of competitive advantage exist — cost, differentiation, and marketing. Choosing the wrong one, or pursuing all three simultaneously, produces worse outcomes than focused commitment. The Prisoner's Dilemma in Chapter 6 makes this concrete and mathematical.

The module is analytically demanding. Variable Cost Advantage Indices, importance-weighted Differentiation Advantage scores, Relative Market Share, and Industry Attractiveness across six Porter forces — all calculations feed the Vantage Industrial case, which requires a specific, quantitatively grounded strategy recommendation. Students who do not complete the apps before the case will not have the numbers the case requires.

⚠ Prerequisite Check — M03–M05 Market Analysis Sequence

This module requires fluency with market share concepts (M03) and segment attractiveness analysis (M05). The Industry Attractiveness Index in this module is structurally parallel to the Segment Attractiveness Scorer from M05 — six forces, weighted scoring, index interpretation. If you have not completed M03–M05, review the key metrics before starting Gate 2.

Learning Objectives
LO1
Evaluation
Assess a firm's most defensible source of competitive advantage given its cost structure, product performance, and market position; explain why pursuing all three sources simultaneously undermines competitive clarity.
LO2
Application
Calculate a Variable Cost Advantage Index, a Differentiation Advantage score using importance-performance analysis, and a Relative Market Share index; interpret each metric's strategic implication.
LO3
Analysis
Distinguish the three sub-types of marketing advantage (market share, product line, channel); compute Relative Market Share; explain the mechanism linking market dominance to profitability.
LO4
Analysis
Analyze the Prisoner's Dilemma payoff structure; identify the Nash equilibrium outcome; explain why mutual price-cutting destroys industry profit even though it is individually rational for each firm.
LO5
Application
Classify competitor behaviors as signals of financial distress or marketing leadership failure; distinguish competitive benchmarking (outside industry) from competitor benchmarking; describe the three-step benchmarking process.
LO6
Synthesis
Score industry attractiveness across six Porter forces; compute an Industry Attractiveness Index; predict profit potential and recommend whether to enter, hold, or exit a competitive environment.

What the Chapter Covers Beyond the Slide Deck

Gate 1 tests these topics. They do not appear on the slides. You must find them in Chapter 6 before taking Gate 1.

  • 1
    Customer Value Map & Fair Price Line
    pp. 191–192 + Appendix 6.1 — Customer value = Fair price − Actual price. A brand priced above the fair price line delivers negative customer value, predicting defection and profitability decline.
  • 2
    Prisoner's Dilemma Payoff Structure
    pp. 218–219, Fig 6-24 — When both competitors cut price simultaneously, total industry contribution falls below the "both hold" outcome. Each firm is individually rational but collectively destructive.
  • 3
    Competitive Benchmarking (Non-Competitor)
    pp. 215–216 — Three-step process: identify weakness → find world-class performer outside the industry → adapt their process. Xerox used American Express; General Mills used hospital food service.
  • 4
    Competitor Intelligence Signals (Figure 6-17)
    p. 211, Fig 6-17 — Two distinct lists: behaviors signaling cash-flow pressure (financial distress) vs. behaviors signaling lack of marketing leadership. Each opens a different strategic window.
  • 5
    Sustainable Advantage Erosion
    pp. 194–195 — GM, NBC, and Sears each lost dominant positions not by cutting quality or raising prices, but because external competitive forces overtook them. Grove: "Only the Paranoid Survive."

Before You Begin — Study Tips
  • Read Chapter 6 actively. The slide deck and lecture video deliberately leave out five topics that Gate 1 tests — including the Customer Value Map, the Prisoner's Dilemma payoff structure, and the two-list competitor intelligence framework. Find all five before you take Gate 1.
  • The advantage selection problem is not theoretical. The case data will point to one source clearly if you run the numbers. Students who arrive at the case without completed VCAI and Differentiation Index calculations from App 1 cannot make a defensible recommendation.
  • Complete App 1 before App 2. The VCAI and Differentiation Index outputs from App 1 feed directly into the competitive position framing in App 2's Industry Attractiveness analysis.
  • Show your calculation steps. The case rubric requires the VCAI formula with inputs, the importance-performance table for the Differentiation Index, and the IAI scoring grid — not just final numbers.
  • One result in the Vantage Industrial case will surprise you. The lecture warns you this is coming. When you see it, go back to the Prisoner's Dilemma logic in LO4 — the surprise is explained there.
