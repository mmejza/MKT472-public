The Advantage Selection Problem
Central Question This Module Answers

Given a firm's actual cost structure, product performance, and market position — which source of competitive advantage should it commit to, and what happens when it refuses to choose?

This module develops your capacity to analyze competitive position analytically — not as a list of strategic options but as a set of measurable, quantifiable advantages with predictable profit implications.

The central intellectual challenge is the advantage selection problem: you will be presented with three legitimate sources of competitive advantage (cost, differentiation, marketing) and shown that choosing the wrong one — or pursuing all three — produces worse outcomes than focused commitment to the one that fits the firm's actual data. The Prisoner's Dilemma in Chapter 6 makes this concrete: firms that misread competitive dynamics destroy industry-wide profit in pursuit of individual gain.

01

What is our source of competitive advantage — and is it actually defensible given the data?

02

Who are our real competitors, and how do we measure the gaps that actually matter?

03

Is this industry worth competing in at all — or are we fighting over a shrinking pool?

This module is analytically demanding. You will compute Variable Cost Advantage Indices, calculate Differentiation Advantage scores using importance-weighted performance ratings, derive Relative Market Share, and score Industry Attractiveness across six Porter forces. All calculations feed a case that requires a specific, quantitatively grounded strategy — not generic advice.

What Chapter 6 Covers Beyond the Slides

Gate 1 tests five topics that appear only in Chapter 6 — not in the slide deck, not in the lecture video. Hunt for these while you read.

Five Topics Gate 1 Will Test NOT IN SLIDES
1
Customer Value Map & Fair Price Line
Customer value = Fair price − Actual price. A brand priced above the fair price line delivers negative customer value, predicting defection and profitability decline. The value map is the diagnostic tool.
pp. 191–192 + Appendix 6.1
2
Prisoner's Dilemma Payoff Structure
When both competitors cut price simultaneously, total industry contribution falls below the "both hold" outcome. Each firm is individually rational but collectively destructive. This is not a theoretical curiosity — it is how real price wars begin.
pp. 218–219, Fig 6-24
3
Competitive Benchmarking (Non-Competitor)
A three-step process: identify a competitive weakness → find a world-class performer outside the industry → adapt their process. Xerox used American Express; General Mills used hospital food service. Different from competitor benchmarking.
pp. 215–216
4
Competitor Intelligence Signals (Figure 6-17)
Two distinct lists: behaviors signaling a competitor is under cash-flow pressure (financial distress) versus behaviors signaling lack of marketing leadership. Each list opens a different strategic window. Knowing which is which is the skill.
p. 211, Fig 6-17
5
Sustainable Advantage Erosion
GM, NBC, and Sears each lost dominant competitive positions — not by cutting quality or raising prices, but because external competitive forces overtook them. Grove's core message: "Only the Paranoid Survive." Competitive monitoring is a management discipline, not a periodic exercise.
pp. 194–195

Study Focus Guide — 8 Questions to Answer While Reading

Answer these questions as you read Chapter 6. They are drawn directly from Gate 1 and Gate 2 targets. Students who complete these eight questions before viewing the slides consistently perform better on both gates.

8 Reading Questions — Chapter 6
Complete these while reading. Do not skip ahead to check answers — work through the chapter to find them.
  1. According to Figure 6-2, what is the relationship between a firm's primary source of competitive advantage and its pre-tax ROI? What happens to ROI when a firm has no clear advantage? Gate 1
  2. Define customer value using the fair price line concept. If a brand's actual price is above the fair price line, what does this predict about customer retention and profitability? Gate 1
  3. What is the Variable Cost Advantage Index? Write the formula. What does an index value below 100 mean? What does a value above 100 mean? Gate 2
  4. In an importance-performance differentiation analysis, when does a firm earn importance points for a given customer benefit? What is the threshold rule, and why is it necessary? Gate 2
  5. Explain the Prisoner's Dilemma in a two-competitor pricing context. Identify the Nash equilibrium. Why do both firms cut price even though both end up worse than if both had held? Gate 1
  6. List the six Porter forces. For each force, identify whether a high or low level of that force makes an industry more attractive. Pay special attention to exit barriers — the direction surprises most people. Gate 2
  7. What is the difference between competitive benchmarking and competitor benchmarking? Describe the three-step competitive benchmarking process. Why benchmark outside the industry? Gate 1
  8. Find Figure 6-17. List two behaviors that signal a competitor is in financial distress and two that signal a lack of marketing leadership. What is the key difference in how you should respond to each? Gate 1
