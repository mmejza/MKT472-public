Competitive Position and Sources of Advantage

Which advantage is actually yours - and what happens when you refuse to choose?

Part III · Competitive Strategy 195 points Two-Gate Accountability
Central Question
What this module asks you to figure out
Which advantage is actually yours - and what happens when you refuse to choose?

This module develops the capacity to analyze competitive position analytically - not as a list of strategic options, but as measurable, quantifiable advantages with predictable profit implications. Every claim in a competitive strategy must be grounded in a number: a Variable Cost Advantage Index, a Differentiation Advantage score, a Relative Market Share ratio, an Industry Attractiveness Index.

The central intellectual challenge is the advantage selection problem: three legitimate sources of competitive advantage exist - cost, differentiation, and marketing. Choosing the wrong one, or pursuing all three simultaneously, produces worse outcomes than focused commitment. The Prisoner's Dilemma in Chapter 6 makes this concrete and mathematical.

i
Why this matters

Sustainable competitive advantage requires clarity and commitment. Unfocused strategies fail not because they lack merit individually, but because pursuing all three sources simultaneously creates ambiguity about how to compete and what customers should expect.

Analysis

    Customer Value Map & Fair Price Line
    pp. 191-192 + Appendix 6.1 - Customer value = Fair price - Actual price. A brand priced above the fair price line delivers negative customer value, predicting defection and profitability decline.
  • 2

    pp. 194-195 - GM, NBC, and Sears each lost dominant positions not by cutting quality or raising prices, but because external competitive forces overtook them. Grove: "Only the Paranoid Survive."

Two Traps to Watch For

This module contains two recurring calculation and strategy errors that students make when moving too quickly from competitive theory to implementation. Both are visible in the chapter material and are reinforced by the apps and case.

Pursuing all three advantage sources simultaneously

The advantage selection problem is real. Choosing cost leadership, differentiation, and marketing advantage all at once creates unfocused operations, confused customer positioning, and conflicting resource priorities.

Where to find it: Chapter 6 pp. 203-206; the Prisoner's Dilemma payoff structure; Vantage Industrial case framing.

Trusting market share or position without validating competitive advantage

High share without clarity on cost, differentiation, or marketing advantage is fragile. The Prisoner's Dilemma shows why dominant position can be eroded by mutual competitive pressure if the advantage is not actively maintained.

Where to find it: Competitor intelligence signals, sustainable advantage erosion cases (GM, NBC, Sears), and Vantage Industrial's competitive environment.
