Life-Cycle Value Pricing — NPV of Ownership Savings

Gate 1 — Q1 LO2 pp. 275–276, Fig. 8-7 Not on slides
BioTronics prices its medical apparatus at $60,000 — $10,000 more than the competitor's $50,000. The BioTronics unit saves the customer $7,000 per year in operating costs over a 5-year product life. At a 20% discount rate, the NPV of those savings is approximately $10,930. Is the $10,000 price premium justified? What is the maximum premium BioTronics could charge while still delivering positive economic value?
What to find in the chapter
  • The definition of Life-Cycle Value Pricing and how it extends value-in-use by applying a discount rate to future ownership savings (p. 275)
  • How to interpret the comparison: premium ($10,000) vs. NPV of savings ($10,930). When premium < NPV, the higher-priced product is the better economic value for the customer (Fig. 8-7)
  • The maximum justified premium rule: the NPV of ownership savings is the ceiling. At exactly that premium the customer breaks even; below it they save money (p. 276)
  • How life-cycle value pricing differs from simple value-in-use: it discounts multi-year savings to present value, accounting for the time value of money (pp. 273–276)
pp. 275–276, Fig. 8-7

Perceived-Value Pricing — Benefits Index and Target PVI

Gate 1 — Q2 LO1 · LO3 pp. 276–278, Fig. 8-8 & 8-9 Not on slides
A business calculates three customer benefit indexes: Product Performance = 120, Service Quality = 127, Brand Reputation = 134. The weighted overall Customer Benefits Index is 123. The business tests prices between $5,000 and $6,250. Both $6,000 and $6,250 produce a Perceived Value Index of approximately 23. Why did the business choose $6,250, and what does a target PVI of ~25 mean strategically?
What to find in the chapter
  • The three dimensions of the Customer Benefits Index — product performance, service quality, brand reputation — and how they are weighted by relative importance (Fig. 8-8, p. 277)
  • What an index of 123 means: the business delivers 23% more perceived benefits than competitors on a weighted basis (not 23% more in product performance alone)
  • The target Perceived Value Index of approximately 25: why the business aims for ~25, not the maximum possible — a PVI far above target means the business is underpricing (Fig. 8-9)
  • The three components of the Cost of Purchase Index: price of equipment, service and maintenance costs, and depreciated value — all customer-borne costs, not seller costs (Fig. 8-9, p. 278)
  • Why the business chose $6,250 over $6,000 when both produced the same PVI (~23): the higher price delivers the same customer value at a better margin (p. 277)
pp. 276–278, Fig. 8-8 & 8-9

Reduce-Focus Pricing — Intentional Share Loss to Improve NMC

Gate 1 — Q3 LO4 pp. 289–290, Fig. 8-21 Not on slides
A chemical company's paint division has five product lines, a 20.5% average margin, and 11.7% market share. Three product lines generate negative Net Marketing Contribution. After implementing a pricing strategy, share falls from 11.7% to 8.6% — but NMC more than doubles from $16M to $36.1M. Was this a success or failure? How does this strategy differ from harvest pricing, and at what life-cycle stage is it used?
What to find in the chapter
  • The definition of reduce-focus pricing: raises prices to shed price-sensitive customers in maturity, improving margins and NMC even as share declines — share loss is the mechanism, not the failure (pp. 289–290)
  • The chapter's exact data: share 11.7% → 8.6%; NMC $16M → $36.1M; average margin 20.5% → 32.1%; volume 125M → 92.1M units (Fig. 8-21)
  • Why margins rise when share falls: price increases drive away the least profitable price-sensitive customers; remaining customers pay more and are more profitable
  • How reduce-focus differs from harvest pricing: reduce-focus is a maturity strategy that finds a profitable price-volume combination and stays there; harvest pricing is a decline strategy that progressively raises prices toward market exit (pp. 289–291)
  • The PLC stage: reduce-focus is positioned at maturity in Fig. 8-15 — not decline, not growth
pp. 289–290, Fig. 8-15 & 8-21
This topic does not appear on slides — read the chapter section carefully

Harvest Pricing — Niche Discovery Through Sequential Price Increases

Gate 1 — Q4 LO4 pp. 290–291, Fig. 8-22 Not on slides
An auto components manufacturer has zero gross profit late in the product life cycle. It raises price 15% to $11.50 and loses 30% of volume. A second 10% increase to $12.65 causes only modest volume loss. A third 10% increase to $13.92 causes no volume loss at all. What does volume stabilization reveal? Why does each successive price increase cause less volume loss than the one before it?
What to find in the chapter
  • The harvest pricing sequence and what it's designed to do: raise prices in decline to progressively reduce volume, eventually exiting the market (pp. 290–291)
  • The niche-discovery twist: at some price point, volume stabilizes because the remaining customers are inelastic enough to absorb the premium — the business has uncovered a profitable niche worth managing, not exiting (p. 291)
  • The exact gross profit at the $13.92 price: margin per unit = $3.92 × 6 million units = $23.52 million — significantly above zero (Fig. 8-22)
  • Why successive increases cause less volume loss: price-sensitive customers leave after the first increase; the remaining base is less elastic and less likely to defect at further increases
  • The correct strategic conclusion after niche discovery: manage the niche at the discovered price-volume combination — do not continue raising prices toward exit
pp. 290–291, Fig. 8-22

Break-Even Market Share vs. Break-Even Volume

Gate 1 — Q5 LO5 pp. 297–298, Fig. 8-28 Not on slides
A business has fixed expenses of $50 million per year, a margin of $2 per unit, and market demand of 200 million units. Calculate break-even volume and break-even market share. The business currently holds 24% market share. A competitor's strategy threatens to cause a 15-point share loss. Should the business be concerned? Why is break-even market share a better risk metric than break-even volume?
What to find in the chapter
  • The break-even volume formula: Fixed Expenses ÷ Margin per Unit = $50M ÷ $2 = 25 million units (p. 297)
  • The break-even market share formula: Break-Even Volume ÷ Market Demand = 25M ÷ 200M = 12.5% (pp. 297–298)
  • The safety cushion: current share (24%) − break-even share (12.5%) = 11.5 percentage points. A 15-point loss would drop share to 9% — below the 12.5% threshold, resulting in a loss
  • Why market share is a superior risk metric: it is constrained between 0 and 100%, making the cushion interpretable in context. Break-even volume is unconstrained and harder to assess without knowing market size (p. 297)
pp. 297–298, Fig. 8-28
Not covered in slides — chapter-exclusive for Gate 1

Economic Value — Total Cost of Ownership Calculation

Gate 2 — Q3 LO2 pp. 273–276, Fig. 8-6
A packaging material supplier charges $2.20 per pound — $0.20 more than the competitor's $2.00. A total-cost-of-ownership analysis shows: Competitor shipping $0.07, handling $0.10, inventory $0.05, usage $3.50, QC $0.03, disposal $0.10. Supplier: shipping $0.05, handling $0.13, inventory $0.01, usage $2.80, QC $0.01, disposal $0.02. Calculate total ownership cost for each product, customer savings per pound, and whether the $0.20 premium is financially justified.
What to find in the chapter
  • The total cost of ownership framework (Fig. 8-5): purchase price is only the tip of the iceberg — acquisition, usage, maintenance, quality control, and disposal costs are the hidden majority (p. 273)
  • How to build the side-by-side ownership cost table (Fig. 8-6): sum all cost categories including purchase price for each product, then compute savings per category and total customer savings
  • The premium justification rule: the firm can charge the $0.20 premium and still save the customer $0.75 per pound — and could theoretically charge up to $0.75 more before the economic value disappears
  • The chapter's specific numbers: Competitor TCO ≈ $6.00/lb; Firm TCO ≈ $5.25/lb; customer saves $0.75/lb despite paying $0.20 more at purchase (Fig. 8-6)
🔢
Gate 2 Q3 will use different numbers but the same table structure. Practice computing the totals from Fig. 8-6 row by row — the method is what you need, not the specific dollar amounts.

Break-Even Percentage — Price Cut and Price Increase Scenarios

Gate 2 — Q4 & Q5 LO5 pp. 294–298, Slides 37–46
A company prices a product at $100 with a variable cost of $60 (contribution margin $40 = 40%). Management proposes a 10% price cut. Scenario A: What minimum percentage volume increase is required to maintain gross profit? Scenario B: If price were raised 10% instead, what is the maximum allowable volume decrease while maintaining gross profit? What does the comparison of these two numbers reveal about the direction of pricing in an inelastic market?
What to find in the chapter (and slides)
  • The break-even % formula: BE% = −$ΔCM ÷ New $CM, where $ΔCM = change in contribution margin per unit (covered in slides 37–43)
  • Scenario A (price cut −10%): New price = $90; new CM = $30; BE% = $10 ÷ $30 = 33.3% required volume increase
  • Scenario B (price increase +10%): New price = $110; new CM = $50; BE% = $10 ÷ $50 = 20.0% maximum allowable volume decrease
  • The inelastic market insight from Fig. 8-27: when elasticity is between 0 and −1, raising price always improves gross profit — the volume loss is smaller than the margin gain. Lowering price always destroys gross profit in an inelastic market
  • The chapter's Yellow Pages example (pp. 294–295): elasticity −0.7, price cut to $90 → gross profit falls from $50M to $42.8M; price raise to $110 → gross profit rises to $55.8M
⚠️
Common error: Students use the original CM as the denominator instead of the new CM. Always compute the new CM after the price change first, then apply the formula. Gate 2 Q4 and Q5 are separate questions for the cut and raise scenarios — practice both.
pp. 293–298, Fig. 8-27 · Slides 37–46

Price Elasticity Direction — When Raising Price Improves Profit

Gates 1 & 2 — Q6 & Q8 LO6 pp. 293–296, Fig. 8-27
A company's price elasticity is estimated at −0.7. Management is debating whether to raise or lower price by 10%. Using Fig. 8-27, predict the effect on gross profit under each direction. Then generalize: when elasticity is inelastic (0 to −0.99), what is always the correct price direction, and why does the opposite feel intuitive to most managers?
What to find in the chapter
  • The price elasticity decision matrix in Fig. 8-27: for each combination of elasticity range (inelastic / unit / elastic) and price direction (raise / lower), the table shows the effect on unit volume, sales revenue, unit margin, and total contribution
  • The inelastic rule: when elasticity is between 0 and −0.99, raising price increases gross profit (volume loss is smaller than margin gain); lowering price decreases gross profit (volume gain is smaller than margin loss). Neither result is intuitively obvious
  • The Yellow Pages worked example (pp. 294–295): elasticity −0.7, current price $100, VC $50. Lower price → GP falls $7.2M. Raise price → GP rises $5.8M. Know both numbers
  • Why it feels counterintuitive: revenue falls when price rises in an inelastic market (volume drops more than price rises). Managers confuse revenue decline with profit decline. They are not the same
  • The elastic case (elasticity −1.01 and beyond): a price decrease can increase gross profit if the volume gain is large enough — but this depends on the margin level and must be verified with the BE% formula
💡
This is the module's central strategic insight. Gate 2 Q6 asks you to apply Fig. 8-27 directly; Gate 2 Q8 asks you to synthesize it with the break-even formula to evaluate the VP of Sales' price-cut proposal in the Vantara case. Master the decision matrix.
pp. 293–296, Fig. 8-27 · Slides 34–37
