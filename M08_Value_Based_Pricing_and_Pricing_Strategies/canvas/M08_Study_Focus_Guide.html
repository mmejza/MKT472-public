Reading Strategy for Chapter 8
📍
Mark the chapter-only figures
Flag Figures 8-7, 8-8, 8-9, 8-21, 8-22, and 8-28 before you start. Gate 1 and Gate 2 both depend on those tables and formulas.
✍️
Write the formulas with the direction rules
Do not just copy the equations. Next to each one, write what the result means for pricing direction, profit, or customer value so the logic is visible when you revisit it.
⚠️
Slow down at the counterintuitive parts
Two places trip students repeatedly: a higher price can be the better value when the NPV of savings exceeds the premium, and price increases can improve gross profit when demand is inelastic. Mark both passages.
🔗
Connect the pricing logic to the slides
Chapter 8 links value pricing, reduce-focus pricing, harvest pricing, and elasticity decisions into one sequence. If you map the sequence now, Gate 2 calculation questions become much easier.
Eight Focus Questions — Answer These While You Read
1
Gate 1 · Chapter Only
Life-Cycle Value Pricing
BioTronics prices its medical apparatus at $60,000 — $10,000 more than the competitor's $50,000. The BioTronics unit saves the customer $7,000 per year in operating costs over a 5-year product life. At a 20% discount rate, the NPV of those savings is approximately $10,930. Is the $10,000 price premium justified? What is the maximum premium BioTronics could charge while still delivering positive economic value?
📄 pp. 275–276, Fig. 8-7
Record the discounted-savings rule and the premium ceiling rule. The premium is justified when it is less than the NPV of savings.
2
Gate 1 · Chapter Only
Perceived-Value Pricing
A business calculates three customer benefit indexes: Product Performance = 120, Service Quality = 127, Brand Reputation = 134. The weighted overall Customer Benefits Index is 123. The business tests prices between $5,000 and $6,250. Both $6,000 and $6,250 produce a Perceived Value Index of approximately 23. Why did the business choose $6,250, and what does a target PVI of ~25 mean strategically?
📄 pp. 276–278, Figs. 8-8 & 8-9
Note the three benefits dimensions, the CBI weight logic, and why the best price is the highest price that still lands near the target PVI.
3
Gate 1 · Chapter Only
Reduce-Focus Pricing
A chemical company's paint division has five product lines, a 20.5% average margin, and 11.7% market share. Three product lines generate negative Net Marketing Contribution. After implementing a pricing strategy, share falls from 11.7% to 8.6% — but NMC more than doubles from $16M to $36.1M. Was this a success or failure? How does this strategy differ from harvest pricing, and at what life-cycle stage is it used?
📄 pp. 289–290, Fig. 8-21
The counterintuitive point is that share loss is the mechanism that improves NMC. Reduce-focus is a maturity strategy, not a decline strategy.
4
Gate 1 · Chapter Only
Harvest Pricing
An auto components manufacturer has zero gross profit late in the product life cycle. It raises price 15% to $11.50 and loses 30% of volume. A second 10% increase to $12.65 causes only modest volume loss. A third 10% increase to $13.92 causes no volume loss at all. What does volume stabilization reveal? Why does each successive price increase cause less volume loss than the one before it?
📄 pp. 290–291, Fig. 8-22
Focus on the niche-discovery twist: stabilization means the remaining customers are inelastic enough to support a profitable niche.
5
Gate 1 · Chapter Only
Break-Even Market Share
A business has fixed expenses of $50 million per year, a margin of $2 per unit, and market demand of 200 million units. Calculate break-even volume and break-even market share. The business currently holds 24% market share. A competitor's strategy threatens to cause a 15-point share loss. Should the business be concerned? Why is break-even market share a better risk metric than break-even volume?
📄 pp. 297–298, Fig. 8-28
Compute the market-share threshold and the safety cushion. The chapter prefers market share because it is bounded and easier to compare with actual share.
6
Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides
Economic Value Calculation
A packaging material supplier charges $2.20 per pound — $0.20 more than the competitor's $2.00. A total-cost-of-ownership analysis shows: Competitor shipping $0.07, handling $0.10, inventory $0.05, usage $3.50, QC $0.03, disposal $0.10. Supplier: shipping $0.05, handling $0.13, inventory $0.01, usage $2.80, QC $0.01, disposal $0.02. Calculate total ownership cost for each product, customer savings per pound, and whether the $0.20 premium is financially justified.
📄 pp. 273–276, Fig. 8-5 & 8-6
Build the ownership-cost table row by row. Gate 2 uses the same structure with different numbers, so focus on the method.
7
Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides
Break-Even Percentage Scenarios
A company prices a product at $100 with a variable cost of $60 (contribution margin $40 = 40%). Management proposes a 10% price cut. Scenario A: What minimum percentage volume increase is required to maintain gross profit? Scenario B: If price were raised 10% instead, what is the maximum allowable volume decrease while maintaining gross profit? What does the comparison of these two numbers reveal about the direction of pricing in an inelastic market?
📄 pp. 293–298, Fig. 8-27 · Slides 37–46
Use the new contribution margin after each price change. The price-cut and price-raise scenarios produce different break-even percentages.
8
Gate 2 · Chapter + Slides
Price Elasticity Direction
A company's price elasticity is estimated at −0.7. Management is debating whether to raise or lower price by 10%. Using Fig. 8-27, predict the effect on gross profit under each direction. Then generalize: when elasticity is inelastic (0 to −0.99), what is always the correct price direction, and why does the opposite feel intuitive to most managers?
📄 pp. 293–296, Fig. 8-27
The central strategic insight is that inelastic demand favors price increases. Rehearse the logic until the direction feels automatic.
🎯 Highest-Probability Gate 1 Targets
If you are short on time, prioritize these three areas — they appear on Gate 1 across all question variants and cannot be answered from the slides alone:
  • Life-cycle value pricing (Q1): Know the discounted-savings rule and the premium ceiling rule.
  • Reduce-focus and harvest pricing (Q3 & Q4): Be able to separate maturity-stage share shedding from decline-stage harvesting, and explain why harvest can reveal a profitable niche.
  • Break-even market share (Q5): Be able to compute the threshold and explain why it is a better risk metric than break-even volume.
