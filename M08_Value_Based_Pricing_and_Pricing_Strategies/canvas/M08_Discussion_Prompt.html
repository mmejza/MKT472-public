Background

Vantara Medical Devices sells a precision diagnostic system at $185,000 per unit. A new competitor has entered the market at a lower price point, and three hospital accounts have gone dark over the past quarter. The VP of Sales wants to cut price. The CFO says the math does not support it.

VP of Sales — Cut Price 12%

“Our price is too high. Competitors are undercutting us on purchase price and we’re losing deals. Three hospitals have gone dark on us in the last quarter. If we drop price by 12%, I’m confident we can grow volume by 30% and hit our revenue target. We need to be competitive or we lose these accounts permanently.”

CFO — Hold Price

“A 12% price cut with our current contribution margin requires significant volume growth just to break even on profit — run the number. Our market elasticity is estimated at −0.9, which is inelastic. At that elasticity, a 12% cut generates around 10–11% volume growth — far short of the 30% the VP is projecting. We’d be destroying margin to chase revenue. The economic value analysis shows customers save money over the ownership period at our current price. We should hold and invest in closing the service quality gap instead.”

Part 2 — Devil's Advocate

Now make the strongest case for the position you just argued against. What is the best evidence the other side has? Where does your own break-even or elasticity argument have a weakness? What conditions would have to be true for the opposing side to be right? (100–150 words)

What Strong Posts Look Like

The best posts show the arithmetic, not just the conclusion. Calculate the new contribution margin at the proposed cut price. Apply the break-even formula to find the minimum volume increase required. Compare that to the elasticity-implied volume gain. Then compare both to the VP’s 30% claim. The gap between what you need and what elasticity predicts is the core of this debate — your post should name that gap explicitly.

Connection to the Case Analysis
