The Central Question
What this module asks you to figure out
If you cut price by 10% and volume grows by 25%, does profit go up or down?

Most managers say up. The data in this module will show you why they're wrong — and what to do instead. By the end, you will have a framework for making pricing decisions that starts with customer value, not production cost, and a set of analytical tools that tell you precisely when to raise price, when to cut it, and when the math says the intuitive move is the wrong one.

The Setup

A company cuts price 10%. Volume grows 25%. Revenue rises 12.5%. The VP of Sales declares success.

→
The Reality

Gross profit falls 16.7%. The margin reduction outweighs the volume gain. The company earned more revenue and lost money doing it.

Why this happens: Breaking even on a 10% price cut at a 30% contribution margin requires a 50% volume increase — not 25%. The intuition is off by a factor of two. This module teaches you to run the calculation before you cut the price, not after you lose the margin.

What the Chapter Covers That the Slides Do Not

Gate 1 tests these five topics. They do not appear on slides or in the lecture video. If you only watch the slides, you will not be able to answer Gate 1 correctly. Read the chapter first.

  • 1
    Life-Cycle Value Pricing (NPV method) Not on slides
    How to discount 5 years of ownership savings to compute economic value; when a price premium is justified by the NPV of ownership cost differences rather than by purchase price alone.
    pp. 275–276, Fig. 8-7
  • 2
    Perceived-Value Pricing Not on slides
    Product performance, service quality, and brand reputation indexes combined into a Customer Benefits Index; how the target Perceived Value Index (~25) determines the optimal price point.
    pp. 276–278, Fig. 8-8 & 8-9
  • 3
    Reduce-Focus Pricing Not on slides
    A maturity-stage strategy that deliberately raises prices to shed price-sensitive customers; market share falls from 11.7% to 8.6% in the chapter example, but Net Marketing Contribution more than doubles.
    pp. 289–290, Fig. 8-21
  • 4
    Harvest Pricing — the niche discovery twist Not on slides
    Sequential price increases in the decline stage; the counterintuitive result when volume stabilizes at a higher price point, revealing a profitable niche worth managing rather than a market to exit.
    pp. 290–291, Fig. 8-22
  • 5
    Break-Even Market Share vs. Break-Even Volume Not on slides
    Why market share (constrained 0–100%) is a superior risk metric to break-even volume (unconstrained); how to calculate the safety cushion between current share and the break-even threshold.
    pp. 297–298, Fig. 8-28
