MAI Calculation — 5-Step Method
LO3 — Application
Figs. 11-9, 11-10 · p. 387
Using Figure 11-10, work through all three dimensions of the MAI calculation from scratch. Verify your results: Market Forces dimension score = 62, Competitive Environment = 44, Market Access = 84, overall MAI = 61.
Why Gate 2 tests this
Gate 2 Q1 gives you a different set of ratings and asks you to compute a dimension score and its weighted contribution — a direct numeric entry question. Practicing the method here on Figure 11-10's known values confirms you understand the sequence before Gate 2 gives you unfamiliar numbers. The most common error: applying the dimension weight to individual metric scores instead of to the dimension score.
What to look for — the 5 steps
Step 1: Metric score = Relative Importance × Attractiveness Rating
Step 2: Dimension score = sum of all metric scores within the dimension
Step 3: Weighted contribution = Dimension Weight × Dimension Score
Step 4: Repeat for all three dimensions
Step 5: MAI = sum of three weighted contributions
Verify your work against these checkpoints
Market Forces: dim score = 62 → 30% × 62 = 18.6  |  Comp Env: dim score = 44 → 40% × 44 = 17.6  |  Market Access: dim score = 84 → 30% × 84 = 25.2  |  MAI = 61.4 ≈ 61

CPI Calculation — Rating Scale Warning
LO4 — Application
Figs. 11-11, 11-12 · pp. 388–389
Using Figure 11-12, work through the CPI calculation. Before you start: note how the CPI rating scale differs from the MAI rating scale. Verify: Differentiation = 74, Cost Advantage = 46, Marketing Advantage = 34, CPI = 55.
Why Gate 2 tests this
Gate 2 Q2 is a parallel CPI calculation — same method as Q1 but different inputs and a different rating anchor. The single most common Gate 2 error is applying the wrong scale: students give a rating of 40 thinking it means "moderately unattractive" (MAI logic) when it actually means "behind competitors" (CPI logic). The scale difference is stated in the chapter and on the slides — Gate 2 checks whether you noticed.
What to look for
CPI scale: 0 = Considerably Behind Competitors · 100 = Considerably Ahead
MAI scale: 0 = Very Unattractive · 100 = Very Attractive — not the same
Why Marketing Advantage dimension scores lowest (34): Distribution rated only 20
Strategic implication: the weakest CPI dimension is the highest-leverage improvement target
Verify your work
Differentiation: dim score = 74 → 40% × 74 = 29.6  |  Cost Adv: dim score = 46 → 40% × 46 = 18.4  |  Mktg Adv: dim score = 34 → 20% × 34 = 6.8  |  CPI = 54.8 ≈ 55
