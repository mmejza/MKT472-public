Eight Focus Questions - Answer These While You Read
1
Gate 1 - Chapter Only
GE/McKinsey multi-factor model
Who developed the multi-factor portfolio matrix, and what specific limitation of single-factor analysis does it address? Name both organizations and state the limitation in one sentence.
Chapter 11 - p. 385
The slides show structure but not the origin detail. Gate 1 often checks this distinction directly.
2
Gate 1 - Chapter Only
Share Development Index (SDI)
Write out the SDI formula and define each component. Then interpret: what does an SDI of 32 mean strategically, versus an SDI of 90?
Formula
SDI = (Market Share Index ÷ Share Potential Index) × 100
Chapter 11 - Fig. 11-5 · pp. 380-381
You need both the math and the interpretation: low SDI signals untapped potential, high SDI signals limited remaining upside.
3
Gate 1 - Chapter Only
Relative market share vs. SDI
How does relative market share differ from a company's absolute market share? How is relative market share calculated? How does it differ from SDI as a measure of competitive position?
Chapter 11 - pp. 378-379
RMS compares the firm against rivals. SDI compares captured share against potential share. Keep those purposes separate.
4
Gate 1 - Chapter Only
Offensive vs. defensive trade-off
In Figure 11-14, the offensive plan produces higher Year-3 NMC than the defensive plan but lower ROS and ROI. Record the Year-3 NMC, ROS, and ROI values for both plans, then explain in one sentence why NMC rises while ROS and ROI fall.
Chapter 11 - Fig. 11-14 · p. 393
This is the central paradox of the chapter. Gate 1 expects exact values and the ratio logic behind them.
5
Gate 1 - Chapter Only
Portfolio diversification levels and variance
Chapter 11 describes two levels of portfolio diversification. What are they? What specific variance data does Figure 11-16 use to demonstrate stability benefits of diversification?
Chapter 11 - Fig. 11-16 · pp. 395-397
Capture the numerical evidence (individual variance values and combined portfolio variance) because Gate 1 variants use those numbers.
6
Gate 2 - Chapter + Slides
MAI calculation - 5-step method
Using Figure 11-10, work through all three dimensions of the MAI calculation from scratch. Verify: Market Forces dimension score = 62, Competitive Environment = 44, Market Access = 84, overall MAI = 61.
Formulas
Metric Score = Relative Importance × Rating
MAI = Σ (Dimension Weight × Dimension Score)
Chapter 11 - Figs. 11-9 and 11-10 · p. 387 · Slides S13-S14
Most common error: applying dimension weight to each metric before calculating dimension score.
7
Gate 2 - Chapter + Slides
CPI calculation and rating-scale warning
Using Figure 11-12, work through the CPI calculation. Before starting, note how CPI's rating scale differs from MAI's. Verify: Differentiation = 74, Cost Advantage = 46, Marketing Advantage = 34, CPI = 55.
Formula
CPI = Σ (Dimension Weight × Dimension Score)
Chapter 11 - Figs. 11-11 and 11-12 · pp. 388-389 · Slides S16-S17
CPI scale is competitor-relative (behind vs. ahead), not attractiveness scale. Mixing scales is the most frequent Gate 2 miss.
8
Gate 2 - Chapter + Slides
Grid placement and zone thresholds
Using Figure 11-13, identify which cell a product with MAI = 61 and CPI = 55 lands in and name strategies for that cell. Then use Figure 11-18 to state numerical thresholds that define Low, Medium, and High zones on both axes.
Chapter 11 - Fig. 11-13 (p. 390) and Fig. 11-18 (p. 399) · Slides S18-S19
Thresholds are critical for borderline cases: Low 0-33, Medium 34-67, High 68-100 on both axes.
