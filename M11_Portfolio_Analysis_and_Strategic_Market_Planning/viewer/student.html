Strategic Market Planning
A forward-looking process for allocating resources across the product portfolio

A process in which firms develop marketing strategies for sustaining and improving short- and long-run performance across their portfolio of products and markets. Portfolio analysis drives it; strategy selection follows from it.

Process flow
Current Performance
NMC · ROS · ROI · Share
→
Portfolio Analysis
MAI · CPI
→
Strategy & Performance Plan
3–5 year horizon
Offensive output
  • Grow sales — existing or new markets
  • Improve or build competitive position
  • Enter new attractive markets
Defensive output
  • Protect or optimize market position
  • Monetize for maximum cash flow
  • Harvest share or divest
Rule

Without this process, resource allocation is intuition — not analysis. A firm cannot know which products to invest in and which to harvest without computing both dimensions.

Business Performance Criteria for Portfolio Review
Three categories — all required before portfolio analysis begins
Market Performance
  • Market share position
  • Sales growth (unit volume)
  • Sales growth (dollar volume)
  • Customer satisfaction / retention
Marketing Profitability
  • Net Marketing Contribution (NMC)
  • Marketing Return on Sales (ROS)
  • Marketing Return on Investment (ROI)
  • Gross profit by segment
Traditional Financial
  • Total revenues
  • Gross profit
  • Operating profit
  • Operating profit %
Rule

Financial metrics alone are backward-looking — they report what happened. Market + marketing profitability metrics reveal competitive trajectory: where the firm is heading, not just where it has been.

Two Approaches to Portfolio Visualization
Both are inputs — neither alone determines strategy
Price-Quality Portfolio
  • Maps products by price point and quality / brand status
  • Reveals competitive white space and positioning gaps
  • Shows where the portfolio is concentrated or missing
Example

Toyota: 3 models in low-price tier (1960s) → 18 models across 3 brands spanning the full price-quality curve (2010)

Product Life Cycle Portfolio
  • Maps products by PLC stage and market growth rate
  • Circle size proportionate to current sales volume
  • Balanced = mix of emerging, growth, and mature stages
  • Unbalanced = products concentrated in mature/declining
Risk signal

All products in mature stages looks stable today — but creates a revenue cliff in 3–5 years when markets decline and nothing replaces them

Bottom takeaway

Both models are inputs to portfolio analysis — neither alone determines strategy. Life-cycle position informs market attractiveness; competitive position requires a separate index. Slides 12–17 build both.

LO2Misconception / Trap
11 / 21
"High Growth Rate = Attractive Market"
Why this intuition is wrong — and what the correct rule is
Common but wrong
"If the market is growing fast, it must be attractive — invest."
Why it seems correct
  • Growth → rising demand → more sales opportunity
  • Product D (20% growth) clearly warrants more attention than Product B (0%)
  • Fast-growing markets produce the biggest future profit pools
Why it is wrong
  • Ignores market size — 20% growth on $10M < 5% growth on $5B
  • Ignores competitive intensity — high growth attracts entrants, compressing margin
  • Ignores market access — distribution barriers make growth unreachable
Correct rule
Market attractiveness requires a multi-factor index across market forces, competitive environment, and market access. Growth rate is one of nine inputs — not the answer.
⚑ Chapter-only

The GE/McKinsey model (p. 385) formalizes this. Gate 1 Q1 tests who developed it and what limitation it addresses — answer is in the chapter, not these slides.

⚠ Critical — most common calculation error
MAI Rating Scale

0 = Very Unattractive market · 100 = Very Attractive market
Measures quality of the market

CPI Rating Scale

0 = Considerably Behind competitors · 100 = Considerably Ahead
Measures standing vs. rivals

Same 0–100 range. Completely different meaning. A rating of 40 in MAI ≠ a rating of 40 in CPI.

Strategic Market Plan vs. Marketing Mix Strategy
Time horizon and performance objective are the primary discriminators
Strategic Market Plan
Time horizon3–5 years
Sets objectives forMarket share position · Sales growth · Long-run NMC
Review cycleAnnually against long-run trajectory
Resource scopeFull portfolio investment allocation
Example

"Grow Beta's market share from 10% to 16% and raise NMC from $12.8M to $24M over 3 years through an Improve Position strategy."

Marketing Mix Strategy
Time horizon1 year
Specifies tactics forProduct positioning · Pricing · Communications · Channels
Review cycleQuarterly; adjusted as market conditions change
Resource scopeAnnual marketing and sales budget for one product
Example

"Reduce Beta's price 5% and increase digital ad spend by $400K in Q1 to accelerate trial in the B2B segment."

Bottom takeaway

Strategy sets the destination — market share and NMC targets 3–5 years out. Marketing mix is how you navigate to it, adjusted year-by-year. The wrong strategic plan cannot be rescued by a brilliant mix.

Strategic Market Plans — Seven Strategies
Three offensive · Four defensive
Offensive — Attractive markets / Improvable position
Invest to GrowInvest marketing and sales resources to grow market demand or grow market share in an expanding market
Improve PositionStrengthen competitive position in an attractive segment where the firm is currently below its potential
New Market EntryEnter new attractive markets or develop new product-markets; requires large upfront investment, operates at a loss until break-even
Defensive — Mature markets / Established position
Protect PositionProtect market share and competitive advantage in a market the firm dominates; invest to hold, not to grow
Optimize PositionMaximize marketing profit at late-growth or mature stage; optimize price-volume mix, reduce unnecessary spend
MonetizeManage for maximum cash flow in a less attractive market; limit investment while remaining in market
Harvest / DivestExtract short-run profit while exiting (harvest = managed exit; divest = sell or close immediately)

Operational mechanics of Monetize, Harvest, and Divest (pricing decisions, payment terms, exit conditions) — pp. 391–392. Gate 1 V4 Q4 tests the Zi-Tech Acoustics harvest mechanism for Product Line D specifically.
